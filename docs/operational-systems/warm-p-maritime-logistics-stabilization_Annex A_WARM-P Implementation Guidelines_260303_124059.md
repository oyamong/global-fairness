I. War-Preceding Position Backtrace Mechanism
Article 1 – Purpose
The purpose of this mechanism is to analyze abnormal financial positions established immediately prior to military tension or armed action, to identify market-distortion-based excess gains, and to convert such gains into public contributions for the protection of the right to life and global stabilization.

This mechanism shall operate as a preventive and protective public contribution framework.

These Guidelines are advisory in nature. Each Member State may implement them autonomously in accordance with its domestic law and essential security interests.

Article 2 – Triggering Conditions
Analysis may be initiated, based on pre-defined criteria, upon the occurrence of one or more of the following:
1. Official announcement of military action or a large-scale security event;
2. Cancellation or significant surge in maritime war-risk insurance coverage in international markets;
3. Abnormal volatility in energy or defense-related financial instruments.

Article 3 – Scope of Analysis
Transactions meeting the following criteria may be subject to analysis:
• Transactions executed within a default 72-hour period prior to military escalation (adjustable in accordance with domestic law);
• Defense stocks, crude oil futures, energy derivatives, or military-related ETFs;
• Sharp increases in trading volume relative to ordinary market patterns;
• Reasonable indication of potential access to inside information.
Assessment of potential access to inside information shall be conducted on the basis of objective indicators, including trading patterns, institutional position, and scope of information access.

Article 4 – Calculation of Excess Profit
1. The benchmark return shall be determined using the 180-day average volatility and industry benchmarks.
2. Gains exceeding this benchmark shall be defined as “War Premium Excess Profit.”
3. Up to 90% of such excess profit may be classified as a public contribution, subject to proportional and graduated application.
4. To ensure objectivity and proportionality, Member States may, where appropriate, conduct cross-validation through independent financial or quantitative experts, or through dedicated supervisory review teams.
5. The calculation methodology shall distinguish legitimate market risk premiums from war-induced excess profit, and the formula, assumptions, and data scope shall be transparently recorded to a level suitable for post-review disclosure.
The contribution ratio shall take into account the severity of the case, potential access to inside information, market distortion scale, and domestic legal standards.

Member States may consider, within their domestic legal frameworks, temporary public stability contribution schemes or similar levy mechanisms applicable to abnormal excess gains arising from high-risk geopolitical instability.

Such contributions shall not constitute criminal penalties but shall be characterized as public or fiscal measures.

II. Gatekeeper Responsibility Framework
Article 5 – Scope of Application
The framework may apply to:
• Law firms
• Accounting firms
• Investment banks
• Asset management institutions
• Insurance and reinsurance brokers

Article 6 – Responsibility Criteria
Review may be initiated where one or more of the following conditions are met:
1. Design of beneficial ownership concealment structures;
2. Advisory services facilitating sanctions evasion;
3. Gross negligence in failing to address high-risk money laundering indicators;
4. Structuring assistance enabling exploitation of war-related premium gains.

Article 7 – Measures
Possible measures include:
• Removal from the WARM-P Cooperation Whitelist;
• Restriction of access to maritime stabilization support mechanisms;
• Recommendation for classification as high-risk institutions;
• Notification of risk assessment findings to relevant supervisory authorities.
All measures shall be implemented only after independent review, opportunity for response, and appeal procedures.
Non-cooperative or high-risk classifications shall be subject to independent review and periodic reassessment.

III. Political Influence Transparency Rule
Article 8 – Purpose
To enhance transparency regarding potential conflicts of interest between political influence and war-related economic gains.

Article 9 – Disclosure Requirements
Disclosure obligations arise where both of the following conditions are met:
1. Significant political contributions;
2. Award of defense, reconstruction, or maritime infrastructure contracts following military escalation.
The threshold for “significant political contribution” may be defined by Member States with reference to domestic political finance law, average election costs, or proportional GDP-based indicators.

Article 10 – Disclosure Content
Required disclosure may include:
• Political contribution records;
• Government contract awards;
• Profit analysis;
• Conflict-of-interest assessment.

Article 11 – Public Contribution Mechanism
Where repeated conflicts of interest are objectively confirmed through due process, and within the limits permitted by domestic law, a proportion of contract value may be allocated to Track A (Victim Recovery Window), subject to proportionality and due process principles.

Application shall be graduated and respectful of domestic legal frameworks.

IV. Global Beneficial Ownership Mapping Framework
Article 12 – Data Integration Principles
1. Member States are encouraged to cross-analyze:
• FIU financial transaction reports;
• CRS tax information;
• On-chain virtual asset movements;
• Vessel AIS data;
• Insurance and reinsurance contract information.
2.   Custody Safeguards
Where digital assets are confirmed as subject to contribution or recovery, Member States are encouraged to employ multi-signature custody structures or designated supervisory custodians.
3.   Volatility Mitigation
Recovered digital assets may be converted within a reasonable timeframe into fiat currency or stable assets to prevent excessive value fluctuation, with conversion processes subject to audit.
4.   Chain Integrity
On-chain tracing results may be validated through independent blockchain analytics tools or multiple analytical institutions to ensure integrity.

Article 13 – Sovereignty and Data Protection
• Application of data minimization principles;
• Prohibition of purpose limitation violations;
• Encrypted storage;
• Appointment of independent data protection supervisors.

Article 14 – Family and Nominee Tracking
• Control and dominance testing;
• Profit attribution pathway analysis;
• Identification of nominee structures;
• Detection of abnormal inter-affiliate flows.

Article 15 – AI-Based Asset Mapping
• Visualization of global beneficial ownership networks;
• Automated risk signal alerts;
• Detection of large-scale capital movement following escalation events.
Algorithms shall be subject to annual bias and fairness review.

V. Maritime Emergency Stabilization Mode
Article 16 – Activation Conditions
Emergency mode may be activated upon:
1. A 30% or greater surge in maritime war-risk insurance premiums;
2. Significant reduction in route operational rates;
3. Reported delays in essential goods supply.

Article 17 – Priority Support
1. Essential food transport;
2. Medicines and medical equipment;
3. Energy imports for vulnerable countries;
4. Humanitarian relief cargo.

Article 18 – Support Conditions
• Maintenance of AIS transparency;
• Provision of cargo and contract information;
• Confirmation of no material violations of international financial transparency standards (including FATF standards).

VI. Procedural Safeguards
Article 19 – Due Process
• Prior notification;
• Evidence disclosure;
• Opportunity to respond;
• Independent review panel examination;
• Right to appeal.

VII. International Cooperation
Article 20 – Cooperation Institutions
• UNODC
• FATF
• Egmont Group
• IMO
• World Bank and IMF

VIII. Evaluation and Reporting
Article 21 – Annual Review
Evaluation may include:
• Volume of recovered assets;
• Victim support implementation status;
• Maritime stabilization effectiveness;
• Financial institution cooperation levels;
• Data protection compliance;
• Assessment of proportionality and due process adherence.
