WARM-P Technical and Data Architecture & Implementation Infrastructure Design

I. Data Protection and Sovereignty Safeguards
This architecture is not designed to establish a mass surveillance system nor to centrally collect personal data. It is structured as a decentralized risk-analysis model that respects State sovereignty and data protection principles.

Article 1 – Data Minimization Principle
1. WARM-P shall collect and process only the minimum data necessary to achieve its stated objectives.
2. Broad mass data pooling is prohibited.
3. Personally identifiable information shall, where feasible, be pseudonymized or anonymized prior to processing.

Article 2 – Purpose Limitation Principle
1. Collected data shall be used solely for identifying assets reasonably linked to the protection of the right to life and high-risk geopolitical instability.
2. Secondary use for taxation, political, or commercial purposes is prohibited.
3. Any change of purpose shall require approval from an independent oversight body.

Article 3 – Data Sovereignty
1. Source data shall remain within each Member State.
2. No centralized global database shall be established.
3. Cross-border sharing shall be limited to risk signals and anonymized analytical outputs.
4. No international organization or coordinating body shall possess any technical or structural backdoor enabling direct access to source data without explicit prior written authorization from the Member State concerned.

Article 4 – Encryption and Security
1. All data transmission shall apply end-to-end encryption (E2EE).
2. Stored data shall be protected under advanced encryption standards.
3. Access shall be restricted through multi-factor authentication (MFA).

Article 5 – Independent Oversight
1. Independent data protection supervisors shall be designated.
2. Annual Privacy Impact Assessments (PIA) shall be conducted.
3. Algorithmic bias and discrimination assessments shall be mandatory.

Article 6 – Legal Nature of Risk Information
1. Risk Scores and analytical outputs generated by the system constitute advisory intelligence.
2. Final freezing, confiscation, or enforcement decisions remain solely under the independent authority of Member States’ judicial or administrative bodies.
3. Legal responsibility for enforcement actions rests with the Member State implementing such actions.
4. This mechanism does not create legal guarantees or joint liability at the level of any international organization.
5. International organizations shall not bear liability for indirect damages arising from system errors, technical malfunctions, or data incompleteness, except in cases of willful misconduct or gross negligence. However, they shall take reasonable measures to comply with the security, audit, PIA, and abuse-prevention safeguards outlined herein.
6. Repeated disregard of risk signals or failure to respond reasonably may be assessed as a deficiency in the relevant institution’s or State’s risk management framework.

II. Design Principles
• Alignment with the protection of the right to life
• Respect for State sovereignty and domestic law
• Guarantee of due process
• Integrity of tamper-resistant record systems
• Compatibility with existing international financial infrastructures
• Prohibition of use as centralized surveillance infrastructure

III. System Architecture
Four-Layer Architecture
Layer 1 – Decentralized Data Nodes
Data remains stored within Member States.
Potential integration sources include:
• FIU STR/CTR reports
• CRS tax data
• On-chain virtual asset data
• Vessel AIS data
• Insurance and reinsurance contract data
• Securities and public disclosure data
No cross-national integrated database shall be created.

Layer 2 – Analytical and Risk Scoring Layer
• Suspicious transaction detection algorithms
• Pre-war position analysis module
• Beneficial ownership network mapping
• Political contribution–contract correlation analysis
• War premium profit calculation engine
Only risk scores and structured risk signals are shared centrally.

Layer 3 – Enforcement Linkage Layer
• Provisional action review API
• Member State enforcement agency interface
• Confiscation procedure management module
• ISDS documentation support module
All enforcement actions remain under Member State sovereignty.

Layer 4 – Transparency and Audit Layer
• Blockchain-based integrity ledger
• Audit log tracking system
• Public reporting dashboard
Personal identifying data shall not be publicly disclosed.
The integrity ledger functions solely to ensure procedural transparency and record immutability and does not constitute enforcement authority.

IV. Data Flow Process
Step 1 – Risk Event Registration
Military action or major security event occurs.

Step 2 – Domestic Node Analysis
Automated analysis occurs within each Member State without cross-border data transfer.

Step 3 – Risk Signal Sharing
Only risk scores and transaction typologies are shared; no personal data is transferred.

Step 4 – Autonomous Enforcement
Member States decide freezing or confiscation actions within domestic legal frameworks.

Member States retain sovereign emergency control authority to temporarily suspend data-sharing functionality in the event of significant cyber intrusion, system misuse, data integrity threats, or national security emergencies. Such suspension shall be recorded, and resumption shall be determined by the Member State concerned.

V. War Premium Calculation Engine
• Excess profit calculations shall be conducted within Member States’ tax or financial authorities.
• The central system provides calculation logic guidance only.
The calculation logic may serve as a technical reference model where Member States design temporary public stability contribution or analogous levy mechanisms.

VI. Beneficial Ownership Mapping – Distributed Verification Model
1. Logical data linkage may reference:
• CRS
• FATCA
• FIU reports
• Corporate registries
• On-chain addresses
Actual source data remains domestically stored.

2.   Control tests:
Risk level increases where two or more dominance indicators are met. Final determination of ultimate control remains within judicial authority of Member States.
3.   Nominee detection may include:
• Repetitive structural patterns
• Circular fund flows
• Repeated use of identical legal representatives
Only risk signals are shared.

VII. Gatekeeper Risk Evaluation Module
Indicators may include:
• Ratio of high-risk client engagements
• Transactions linked to sanctioned entities
• History of high-risk structure design
• AML warning non-response records
Risk scores above 80 may trigger a “High-Risk Recommendation.”
Risk scoring functions as an advisory indicator and shall be assessed alongside qualitative supervisory criteria. Final sanctions remain at Member State discretion.

VIII. Maritime Stabilization Analytical System
• Detection of war-risk insurance premium surges (typically 30% above 12-month averages, considering historical volatility and structural market factors; adjustable by Member States).
• Route risk scoring
• Automated prioritization of essential goods support
All support measures operate under transparency conditions.

IX. International Interface
1. Potential Institutional Linkages
Information exchange may consider coordination with:
• UNODC
• FATF
• Egmont Group
• IMF
• IMO
APIs are structured around risk signals only; no source data transfer occurs.

2. Market and Supervisory Linkages
Risk signals and cooperation grading may serve as reference indicators in:
1) Maritime war-risk coverage and reinsurance assessments
2) Freight rate and risk premium evaluation
3) Financial institution risk-weight calculations
4) Supervisory prioritization decisions
5) Risk-based trade finance and settlement reviews

3. Integration with National Risk Frameworks
Outputs may inform:
• National Risk Assessments (NRA)
• Risk-Based Approach (RBA) supervisory systems
• Beneficial Ownership transparency evaluations
• AML supervisory enhancement measures

4. Relationship with Existing Systems
This architecture does not replace:
• National Risk Assessment systems
• Beneficial ownership registries
• AML supervisory frameworks
• Maritime war-risk insurance models
• Early warning systems
It is designed as a technical complement to enhance interoperability and efficiency.

Risk signal exchange may reference ISO 20022 extended messaging structures or equivalent international data standards to ensure interoperability. Adoption of such standards remains advisory and subject to domestic system compatibility.

X. Audit and Abuse Prevention
1. External independent audit
2. Algorithm transparency reporting
3. Political misuse review committee
4. Annual structural improvement reporting
5. Algorithm Verification Sandbox involving Member States, technical experts, and civil society
6. Public summary reports with protection of sensitive data
7. Transparent cooperation grading criteria
8. Functional separation between risk generation, enforcement decisions, and allocation/accounting
9. Explainable AI (XAI) compliance, enabling enforcement authorities to understand core risk factors (e.g., transaction patterns, connectivity strength, cross-signal frequency) without disclosure of proprietary source code or sensitive security components

XI. Expandability
The architecture may be extended to:
• Environmental accountability
• Large-scale human rights violations financing
• Sanctions evasion monitoring
All extensions shall adhere to the same data protection and sovereignty safeguards.

This technical architecture is advisory in nature. Member States determine implementation scope according to domestic legal and technical conditions. Participation is voluntary and flexible.
