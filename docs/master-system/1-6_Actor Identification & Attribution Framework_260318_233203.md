PHASE 1: Operational Detection & Response Infrastructure
1-6. Actor Identification & Attribution Framework

Submitted by: Jungyun Kwon, Citizen of the Republic of Korea and Global Citizen
Date: March 19, 2026 (KST)

Article 1. Purpose
① This regulation establishes the criteria and procedures for identifying actors involved in acts of aggression and attributing responsibility.
② The purposes of this regulation are as follows:
• Identification of the actual actors involved in acts of aggression
• Support for determining state responsibility
• Analysis of proxy conduct and indirect attack structures
• Establishment of responsibility structures for international judicial proceedings
③ This regulation is linked to the following documents:
• Definition of Aggression
• Aggression Determination Framework
• Aggression Evidence & Monitoring Protocol
④ This regulation operates as an analytical and attribution-support framework, not as a mechanism for final determination of responsibility.
Article 2. Principles of Attribution
① Identification of actors and attribution of responsibility shall be conducted in accordance with the following principles:
• Evidence-based principle
• Technical analysis principle
• Consistency with international law
• Political neutrality principle
② Actor identification shall be based on reasonable evidence and technical analysis.
③ Responsibility shall not be established on the basis of a single piece of evidence alone.
④ Attribution determinations shall be based on structural analysis of combined evidence, rather than probabilistic assessment.
Article 3. Categories of Actors
① Actors that may be involved in acts of aggression shall be classified into the following categories:
• Direct Actors
• Military organizations
• Special operations units
• Organizations executing armed attacks
• Command and Control Actors
• Military command structures
• State leadership
• Operational command institutions
• Supporting Actors
• Military support organizations
• Weapons suppliers
• Intelligence providers
• Proxy Actors
• Militias
• Paramilitary organizations
• Proxy armed groups
• Technical Actors
• Cyber attack organizations
• Organizations operating AI-enabled or autonomous weapons
• Organizations related to satellite-based attacks
② The following may be classified as Participation Actors:
• Actors that do not directly execute aggression
• But enable or facilitate the execution of aggression
③ Participation Actors may include:
• Providers of military support
• Organizations supplying weapons or military logistics
• Providers of financial or funding support
• Providers of intelligence or technical support
• Cyber operational support organizations
• Providers of territory or military infrastructure
④ Participation Actors may overlap within the aggression structure with Facilitation Actors or Supporting Actors.
⑤ The level of responsibility and state attribution of Participation Actors shall be determined in accordance with:
• Responsibility Attribution & Allocation Framework
• State Responsibility Differentiation & Attribution Framework
⑥ A single incident may involve multiple categories of actors simultaneously.
Article 4. State Responsibility
① Conduct may be attributable as State Responsibility where the following conditions are met:
• The act is carried out by state armed forces
• The act is conducted under state direction or control
• The act is carried out with state support or authorization
② State responsibility may also be recognized where the state uses proxy forces.
③ State responsibility may be recognized where the state has the capacity to prevent the conduct but intentionally fails to do so.
④ State responsibility assessments shall remain aligned with the State Responsibility Differentiation & Attribution Framework.
Article 5. Attribution Criteria
① Attribution of actors shall be assessed by considering the following factors comprehensively:
• Command and control relationships
• Military operational patterns
• Use of technical infrastructure
• Communication records
• Financial and material support
• Financial Intelligence and money-laundering flows
• Virtual asset and digital asset transfer structures
• Complex financial networks or indirect funding patterns
② In the case of cyber attacks, the following factors shall be considered:
• Attack infrastructure
• Code structure
• Attack patterns
• Operational purpose
• Funding and operational support channels
③ Analysis of financial and asset flows shall remain aligned with:
• Actor-Based Financial Risk Flagging Framework
• Influence-Linked Asset Monitoring Framework
Article 6. Evidence Requirements
① Attribution determinations shall be based on multiple forms of evidence.
② Recognized evidence may include:
• Satellite observation data
• Military movement records
• Communications analysis materials
• Cyber forensic data
• Field evidence
③ Evidence collection and management procedures shall follow the Aggression Evidence & Monitoring Protocol.
④ Evidence integrity shall remain aligned with the Chain of Custody & Evidence Integrity Framework.
Article 7. Attribution Process
① The process for actor identification and attribution of responsibility shall proceed as follows:
Evidence Analysis
↓
Actor Identification
↓
Command Structure Analysis
↓
State Responsibility Assessment
↓
Preparation of Attribution Report
② Analytical results shall be submitted to the Aggression Determination Council.
③ This process shall remain aligned with the Aggression Determination Framework.
Article 8. Attribution Report
① The results of actor attribution analysis shall be prepared in the form of an Attribution Report.
② The report shall include the following:
• Incident overview
• Results of technical analysis
• Results of actor identification
• Analysis of state responsibility
• List of evidence
③ The report shall be used in aggression determination procedures.
④ The report may be linked to the Responsibility Dossier Generation System.
Article 9. Judicial Use
① Attribution Reports may be used in international judicial proceedings.
② Reports may be transmitted to the following institutions or jurisdictions:
• Domestic judicial authorities
• International Criminal Court (ICC)
• International Court of Justice (ICJ)
• Other competent courts or prosecutorial authorities
③ Reports may be used in determining international criminal responsibility or state responsibility.
④ Submission procedures shall remain aligned with the Multi-Jurisdiction Judicial Submission Protocol.
Article 10. Transparency and Review
① A certain level of disclosure may be made in order to ensure the credibility of attribution analysis.
② Disclosable materials may include:
• Analytical methods
• Technical verification results
• Summary of evidence
③ Where new evidence is discovered, attribution determinations may be reviewed.
④ Disclosure shall be conducted with due regard to the principles of civilian protection and prevention of misuse of information.
Article 11. Relationship with the Global Collective Defense System
① This regulation functions as the responsibility analysis framework of the Global Collective Defense System.
② This regulation is linked to the following documents:
• Aggression Evidence & Monitoring Protocol
• Aggression Determination Framework
• Collective Response Framework
③ This regulation is responsible for the attribution and responsibility-linkage stage within the system.
Article 12. Third-State Infrastructure Safeguards
① Where a cyber attack or remote attack passes through infrastructure located in a third state, that state shall not automatically bear responsibility.
② A third state shall be regarded as a good-faith infrastructure-providing state where the following conditions are met:
• Absence of direct control over the attack infrastructure
• Lack of prior knowledge of the attack
• Reasonable response after the attack
③ Responsibility shall be considered only where the following conditions are established:
• Intentional support
• Prior knowledge
• Intentional failure to fulfill the duty to prevent
④ Responsibility assessment shall be conducted in accordance with technical verification procedures.
⑤ Mere transit through infrastructure shall not establish responsibility.
⑥ This Article shall be interpreted as a standard designed to protect third states against the risk of unintentional infrastructure provision.
Article 13. Good-Faith Infrastructure Compliance
① The following measures shall be regarded as fulfillment of good-faith management obligations:
• Operation of intrusion detection systems
• Cooperation in cyber response
• Cooperation in international investigations
• Blocking of attack infrastructure
② Such a state may be exempt from responsibility.
③ Determination shall be based on technical analysis and records of cooperation.
④ This Article functions as a safeguard for maintaining incentives for international cooperation.
Article 14. Attribution Confidence Levels
① Attribution results shall be classified according to Confidence Level.
② The levels are as follows:
• High Confidence
• Moderate Confidence
• Low Confidence
③ The criteria are as follows:
• High Confidence
• Multiple independent sources of evidence
• Consistency between technical and intelligence analysis
• Clear state linkage
• Moderate Confidence
• Multiple forms of evidence exist
• Some uncertainty remains
• Low Confidence
• Limited evidence
• Further investigation required
④ This classification system shall be used as a core benchmark in determination and response stages.
Article 15. Attribution Confidence Reporting
① The Confidence Level shall be explicitly stated in the Attribution Report.
② The report shall include the following elements:
• Types of evidence
• Analytical methods
• Data sources
• Confidence classification
③ Confidence levels shall be used as reference criteria in the determination process.
④ Reporting formats shall remain aligned with the Legal Evidence Packaging Protocol.
Article 16. False Flag and Misattribution Safeguards
① The possibility of deliberate misattribution (false flag operations) shall be examined.
② The following factors shall be subject to mandatory analysis:
• Inconsistency in attack patterns
• Mixed use of code and tools
• Disguised timing, language, or operational style
• Inconsistency with known prior patterns
• Excessive traces pointing to another state
③ Attribution shall not be established on the basis of a single technical trace alone.
④ Where the possibility of a false flag exists, the confidence level shall be adjusted.
⑤ This Article shall be regarded as a mandatory verification safeguard against misattribution.
Article 17. Confidence-Linked Response Guidance
① The attribution confidence level shall serve as a core criterion for determining the level of response.
② The basic principles are as follows:
• High Confidence: Strong collective response and legal measures may be taken
• Moderate Confidence: Diplomatic measures and limited economic measures
• Low Confidence: Monitoring and further investigation-centered response
③ The exercise of armed self-defense shall be assessed separately in accordance with international law.
④ Expanded or collective responses shall, in principle, be based on High Confidence.
⑤ Responses shall comply with the principles of proportionality, necessity, and civilian protection.
⑥ This Article shall serve as a benchmark in the application of the Collective Response Framework.
