1-8. State Responsibility Differentiation & Attribution Framework

Submitted by: Jungyun Kwon, Citizen of the Republic of Korea and Global Citizen
Date: March 19, 2026 (KST)

Article 1. Purpose
① This framework defines the criteria for distinguishing between individual responsibility and state responsibility, and for determining the applicability and scope of state responsibility in relation to acts of aggression and related conduct.
② The purposes are as follows:
• Establishing the principle of priority of individual responsibility
• Preventing both excessive attribution and evasion of state responsibility
• Building a fair and proportionate responsibility structure
• Ensuring consistency with judicial procedures and reparations frameworks
③ This framework provides conditional and structured criteria for judgment, excluding any automatic presumption of state responsibility.
④ This framework remains aligned with the following documents:
• Actor Identification & Attribution Framework
• Responsibility Attribution & Allocation Framework
• State Responsibility & Reparations Framework
Article 2. Fundamental Principle
① Responsibility shall follow the following structure:
• Individual Responsibility = Primary Liability
• State Responsibility = Conditional State Responsibility
② State responsibility shall not arise automatically.
③ All determinations shall follow the following principles:
• Principle of proportionality
• Principle of due process
• Evidence-based determination
• Human Final Authority Principle
④ At the reparations stage, state responsibility may be extended as follows:
• The state may bear supplementary responsibility for harm not fully satisfied through individual responsibility
• The state may ultimately serve as a Guarantor for the realization of reparations
⑤ This Article does not regard the state as the default actor, and includes the state as a subject of responsibility assessment only where the relevant conditions are met.
Article 3. Scope of State Responsibility
① State responsibility shall be considered only in the following cases:
• Involvement of state institutions or actors exercising public authority
• Conduct carried out under state control
• Conduct of which the state had knowledge and nevertheless tolerated
② The following may be included:
• Government and administrative institutions
• Military and paramilitary organizations
• Organizations under state direction or control
• Entities effectively performing state functions
③ At the reparations stage, state responsibility may be extended to cover the following:
• Harm of a scale that individuals or organizations cannot bear
• Structural or long-term harm
• Social or public losses
④ The scope of state responsibility shall be determined based not only on the conduct itself, but also on the structural linkage of control, knowledge, and failure to prevent.
Article 4. Core Determination Criteria
① Determination of state responsibility shall be based on the following three elements:
1. Control
• High: direct command or instruction
• Medium: indirect control or influence
• Low: no control
2. Knowledge
• Actual Knowledge
• Constructive Knowledge
• No Knowledge
3. Prevention Capability
• Preventable: preventable but not prevented
• Limited: partially preventable
• Not Preventable: realistically not preventable
② These criteria shall apply not only to the determination of responsibility, but also to the assessment of the scope of reparations responsibility.
③ Application of these criteria shall remain aligned with the attribution findings of the Actor Identification & Attribution Framework and the allocation analysis of the Responsibility Attribution & Allocation Framework.
Article 5. Attribution Logic
① State responsibility may be recognized where the following conditions are met:
• Presence of control or knowledge AND
• Failure to prevent or acquiescence
② State responsibility shall be strengthened in the following cases:
• Direct command or approval
• Organized or repeated conduct
• Large-scale harm
③ State responsibility may be limited in the following cases:
• Partial control or indirect involvement
• Limited level of knowledge
④ At the reparations stage, the following structure may apply:
• The state may bear supplementary responsibility for harm not fully satisfied through individual responsibility
• The reparations share may be adjusted according to the level of responsibility
⑤ Where the following conditions are met, a state may be assessed as a Participating State:
• Provision of military, material, financial, or technical support for acts of aggression
• Provision of infrastructure or territory enabling the execution of aggression
• Knowledge of, or reasonable possibility of knowledge of, the act of aggression
⑥ The responsibility of a Participating State shall be assessed according to the following criteria:
• Level and influence of the support
• Degree of contribution within the aggression structure
• Control, knowledge, and capacity to prevent
⑦ Where a Participating State is involved in the course of aggression, that state may be assessed as follows:
• A jointly responsible actor within the aggression structure
• Potentially excluded from protected reparations status for harm arising during the response to aggression
⑧ Such a state may fall within the legitimate scope of defense by the defending state during the response to aggression.
⑨ However, exceptions may apply where the following are established:
• Clear exercise of collective self-defense
• Limited intervention aimed at restraining aggression
• A legitimate defensive purpose under international law
⑩ Application of this Article shall follow the following principles:
• Principle of proportionality
• Principle of necessity
• Principle of civilian protection
⑪ Attribution determinations shall be based not on mere political association, but on structural contribution and legal relevance.
Article 6. State Responsibility Levels
① State responsibility shall be classified into the following levels:
Level 1: Full State Responsibility
• Direct command or planning
• Explicit approval
→ Full reparations and responsibility
Level 2: Partial State Responsibility
• Knowledge or acquiescence
• Indirect support
→ Partial reparations
Level 3: Due Diligence Failure
• Preventable but not prevented
→ Limited responsibility
Level 4: No State Responsibility
• Purely individual conduct
• No state control or knowledge
→ State exempted from responsibility
② At the reparations stage, the following may additionally apply:
• In the case of Level 2 and Level 3, the state may bear supplementary reparations responsibility
• Even in the case of Level 4, a limited guarantee role may exceptionally apply for the protection of public harm
③ Each level may be further specified as to reparations scope and guarantee structure in conjunction with the State Responsibility & Reparations Framework.
④ Where the following conditions exist, a state may be assessed as a Constrained Participating State:
• External military pressure or threat
• Presence of foreign military forces or effective foreign military control
• Constraints arising from existing defense agreements or military cooperation structures
• Political or economic dependency restricting decision-making autonomy
⑤ Where such conditions are recognized, state responsibility may be adjusted as follows:
• Downward adjustment of the level of responsibility
• Reassessment from Full or Partial State Responsibility to limited responsibility or Due Diligence Failure
• Adjustment of the scope of reparations contribution
⑥ The following factors shall be considered in adjusting state responsibility:
• The state’s effective capacity to exercise control
• Level of knowledge concerning the act of aggression
• Prevention capability and actual response
• Measures taken to minimize harm
⑦ Mitigation of responsibility may be restricted where the following conduct is identified:
• Voluntary expansion of participation
• Independent participation in attacks
• Provision of additional military or financial support
⑧ This Article does not imply:
• Automatic exemption from state responsibility
• Exclusion from the aggression structure
⑨ Such a state may be assessed as:
• A participating actor within the aggression structure
• A participating state with limited responsibility
⑩ The final level of responsibility shall be determined in accordance with the following principles:
• Principle of proportionality
• Evidence-based determination
• Consistency with international law
Article 7. Individual Responsibility Priority
① In all cases, individual responsibility shall apply first.
② State responsibility shall not replace individual responsibility.
③ The following conduct shall not be shifted to state responsibility:
• Arbitrary criminal conduct of an individual
• Conduct occurring outside state control
④ Reparations shall follow the following order:
• First: individual responsibility
• Second: supplementary state responsibility
• Third: public or international funds
⑤ This principle operates as a core safeguard against excessive expansion into generalized state responsibility.
Article 8. Safeguards Against Misuse
① The following principles shall apply:
• Prohibition of political retaliation
• Prohibition of shifting responsibility due to a change in government
• Prohibition of collective responsibility
② Excessive attribution of responsibility to a particular state or group is prohibited.
③ The reparations structure must also ensure the following:
• Prevention of excessive burden on innocent populations
• Fair and proportionate allocation
④ This framework shall not be used as an instrument for political stigmatization.
Article 9. Evidence Requirement
① Determination of state responsibility must satisfy the following criteria:
• Objective evidence-based foundation
• Multi-source verification
• Secured Chain of Custody
② State responsibility shall not be recognized on the basis of presumption or mere association alone.
③ The same evidentiary standard shall apply to reparations responsibility.
④ Evidentiary standards shall remain aligned with the following documents:
• Aggression Evidence & Monitoring Protocol
• Chain of Custody & Evidence Integrity Framework
• Legal Evidence Packaging Protocol
Article 10. Interaction with Responsibility Dossier
① Results of state responsibility determination shall be reflected in the Responsibility Dossier.
② The following information shall be included:
• Whether the state was involved
• Level of responsibility
• Linked actors and networks
③ Reparations-related information may also be included:
• Reparations contribution ratio
• Scope of supplementary responsibility
④ This Article is structurally linked to the Responsibility Dossier Generation System.
Article 11. Linkage to Asset Recovery
① Depending on the level of state responsibility, the following may be determined:
• Whether state assets are included
• Scope of reparations
• Priority of recovery
② Where state responsibility is recognized, asset recovery through international cooperation may be pursued.
③ Where individual asset recovery does not provide sufficient reparations, state assets or public resources may be used in a supplementary manner.
④ This Article shall remain aligned with the following documents:
• Judicial Execution & Asset Recovery Protocol
• Provisional Measures & Risk Containment Protocol
• State Responsibility & Reparations Framework
• Victim Impact & Damage Documentation Framework
Article 12. Right to Clarification
① The relevant state shall have the following rights:
• To deny involvement
• To argue for reduction of the scope of responsibility
• To challenge the evidence
② The results of clarification shall be reflected in the level of responsibility.
③ The results of clarification shall also affect the scope of reparations responsibility.
④ Clarification procedures must ensure procedural fairness and recordability.
Article 13. Human Final Authority
① Final determination of state responsibility shall be subject to human review.
② Human decision-makers shall have the following authority:
• To recognize or exclude responsibility
• To adjust the level of responsibility
• To request additional investigation
③ Final decisions concerning reparations shall also be subject to human review.
④ This Article shall remain aligned with the Human Final Authority Principle.
Article 14. Transparency & Auditability
① All determination processes shall be recorded.
② Records shall include the following:
• Basis for determination
• Applied criteria
• Sources of evidence
• Review process
③ Such records shall be used for ex post audit and legal verification.
④ Reparations-related determinations and allocation structures shall likewise be subject to recording and audit.
⑤ Disclosure or sharing shall be conducted with due regard to the principles of civilian protection, diplomatic stability, and prevention of misuse of information.
