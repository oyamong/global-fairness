Global Collective Defense System
PHASE 1: Operational Detection & Response Infrastructure
1-5. Aggression Evidence & Monitoring Protocol

Submitted by: Jungyun Kwon, Citizen of the Republic of Korea and Global Citizen
Date: March 19, 2026 (KST)

Article 1. Purpose
① This regulation establishes the procedures for real-time detection, monitoring, and evidence collection in relation to acts of aggression.
② The purposes of this regulation are as follows:
• Early detection of acts of aggression
• Securing objective evidence
• Supporting rapid aggression determination
• Preserving evidence for international judicial proceedings
③ This regulation is linked to the following documents:
• Definition of Aggression
• Aggression Determination Framework
• Aggression Determination Authority Framework
• Legal Evidence Packaging Protocol
• Chain of Custody & Evidence Integrity Framework
• Actor Identification & Attribution Framework
• Temporal Applicability & Continuing Harm Framework
④ This regulation operates as a non-punitive, technology-based support system, while determination and enforcement shall follow separate procedures.
Article 2. Monitoring System Principles
① The aggression monitoring system shall operate in accordance with the following principles:
• Real-time monitoring principle
• Multi-source data verification principle
• Technology neutrality principle
• International cooperation principle
② Monitoring shall not be restricted by the political interests of any specific state.
③ The monitoring system shall function as a continuously operating global monitoring system, not merely as a post-incident investigative mechanism.
④ The monitoring system shall comply with the principles of proportionality and minimum intrusion.
⑤ This monitoring system shall respect state sovereignty and shall not possess authority for compelled access to data.
Article 3. Global Monitoring Network
① A Global Aggression Monitoring Network may be operated for the purpose of monitoring aggression.
② The monitoring network may utilize the following infrastructure:
• Military and civilian satellite monitoring
• Air and maritime movement monitoring
• Global communications data analysis
• Cyber attack detection systems
③ The monitoring network may cover the following domains:
• Land
• Maritime
• Air
• Cyberspace
• Outer space
④ The monitoring network may operate on the basis of a distributed data architecture.
Article 4. Data Sources
① Data that may be used for aggression monitoring and evidence collection include the following:
• Satellite data
• Earth observation satellite imagery
• Thermal detection data
• Missile launch detection data
• Maritime and aviation data
• AIS maritime movement data
• Aviation radar records
• Military movement monitoring data
• Cyber data
• Network attack logs
• Records of intrusions into national infrastructure
• Malware analysis
• Communications and electronic signals
• Electronic signal detection
• Communications pattern analysis
• Field materials
• Video records
• Photographic records
• Damage reports
② In collecting field materials, the following protective measures shall apply:
• De-identification of evidence providers
• Reduction or anonymization of location data
• Protection of metadata
• Application of whistleblower protection protocols
③ Protection of civilians or insider informants shall be regarded as an essential safety requirement.
④ Data collection shall remain consistent with legal and ethical standards and with international human rights standards.
Article 5. Evidence Collection Standards
① Evidence used for aggression determination shall be verified through at least two independent data sources.
② Evidence collection shall satisfy the following standards:
• Accuracy of time records
• Verification of location data
• Assurance of data integrity
③ The evidence collection process shall be recorded and remain subject to subsequent verification.
④ Evidence collection shall remain aligned with the Legal Evidence Packaging Protocol.
Article 6. Evidence Preservation
① Collected evidence shall be preserved in a manner that enables its use in international judicial proceedings.
② Evidence preservation shall follow the following principles:
• Maintenance of Chain of Custody
• Prevention of data alteration
• Assurance of long-term storage capability
③ Digital evidence may be managed through encrypted storage mechanisms.
④ Evidence preservation shall remain aligned with the Chain of Custody & Evidence Integrity Framework.
Article 7. Evidence Verification
① Collected data shall undergo technical verification procedures.
② Verification methods may include the following:
• Cross-data analysis
• Comparison of satellite imagery
• Signal analysis
• Cyber forensic analysis
③ Verification procedures shall be conducted by the Technical Verification Division.
④ Verification procedures shall be designed to ensure reproducibility and independent verifiability.
Article 8. Event Detection and Alert System
① Where indicators of aggression are detected, an automated alert system may be activated.
② Alert-triggering situations may include the following:
• Large-scale military movements
• Missile launches
• Cyber attacks against national infrastructure
• Territorial intrusion
③ Upon issuance of an alert, the procedures under the Aggression Determination Framework may be initiated.
④ Automated alerts shall be regarded as initial detection signals and not as determinations.
Article 9. Evidence Transmission
① Collected evidence may be transmitted to the following bodies:
• Aggression Determination Authority
• Aggression Determination Council
② After aggression determination, evidence may be transmitted to the following institutions or jurisdictions:
• Domestic judicial authorities
• International Criminal Court (ICC)
• International Court of Justice (ICJ)
• Other competent courts or prosecutorial authorities
③ This Article shall remain aligned with provisions concerning Judicial Evidence Transmission.
Article 10. Transparency and Data Disclosure
① A certain level of data disclosure may be permitted in order to ensure the credibility of the monitoring system.
② Disclosable materials may include the following:
• Satellite imagery
• Military movement analysis
• Damage reports
③ Disclosure may be limited in consideration of the following:
• Protection of military confidentiality
• Protection of civilian safety
④ Disclosure shall be conducted with due regard to preventing misuse of information and maintaining international stability.
Article 11. International Cooperation
① Monitoring and evidence collection systems may operate through international cooperation.
② Cooperation partners may include the following:
• National monitoring agencies
• International organizations
• Research institutions
• Private satellite operators
③ Cooperation shall be carried out pursuant to data-sharing agreements.
④ Cooperation shall be based on mutual trust and principles of data protection.
Article 12. Relationship with the Global Collective Defense System
① This regulation functions as the technology-based infrastructure of the Global Collective Defense System.
② This regulation is directly connected to the following frameworks:
• Aggression Determination Framework
• Aggression Determination Authority Framework
• Collective Response Framework
③ This regulation is responsible for the system’s initial detection and evidence-securing stage.
Article 13. Data Authenticity and Anti-Manipulation Measures
① A verification system shall be operated to prevent forgery or manipulation of data.
② The following forms of data manipulation shall be included as detection targets:
• Deepfake videos or images
• Manipulated satellite imagery
• Signal spoofing
• Alteration of location data
• Manipulation of cyber logs
③ Verification procedures may utilize the following technologies:
• Digital forensic analysis
• Verification of authenticity of video and image materials
• Metadata analysis
• Multi-source cross-verification
• AI-based anomaly detection
④ Algorithmic integrity verification shall be conducted with respect to AI analytical systems and data-processing procedures.
⑤ The following acts shall be regarded as serious threats:
• Data poisoning
• Manipulation of training data
• Attempts to distort analytical algorithms
⑥ Algorithmic integrity verification shall include:
• Verification of training data sources
• Verification of consistency in model outputs
• Cross-validation among independent systems
• Automated alerts based on anomaly detection
⑦ Where data manipulation is confirmed, such material shall not be recognized as evidence.
⑧ This Article shall function as a core safeguard for ensuring informational reliability.
Article 14. Integrity Verification Technologies
① The following technologies may be utilized to ensure the reliability of evidence data:
• Digital signature-based data authentication
• Original satellite data authentication systems
• Blockchain-based evidence recording systems
• Timestamp authentication systems
② All digital evidence shall pass data integrity verification procedures.
③ Integrity verification records may be used in international judicial proceedings.
④ The application of such technologies shall operate on the basis of functional requirements without mandating any specific technology.
Article 15. Private Sector Cooperation Framework
① Cooperation with private companies and research institutions may be undertaken for aggression monitoring and evidence collection.
② Cooperation partners may include the following:
• Private satellite operators
• Cybersecurity companies
• Data analytics firms
• Research institutions and universities
③ Data provided by private companies shall satisfy the following standards:
• Objectivity
• Clarity of data source
• Technical verifiability
④ Cooperation shall prioritize the public interest.
⑤ Commercial interests of enterprises shall not prevail over international security objectives.
⑥ The following acts shall be regarded as serious violations:
• Deletion of data under pressure from a specific state
• Manipulation or concealment of data
• Refusal or distortion of evidence provision
⑦ In relation to violating companies, the following measures may be considered:
• Restriction on participation in international procurement projects
• Exclusion from global cooperation networks
• Downgrading of international trust assessments
⑧ Such cooperation measures shall be applied not as sanctions, but as trust-based adjustments to participation conditions.
Article 16. Safeguards for Private Data Contribution
① Data contributed by private actors shall be protected under the following principles:
• Legal protection of data providers
• Protection of information sources
• Protection of commercial confidentiality
② Provided data shall be used solely for the following purposes:
• Aggression monitoring
• Aggression determination procedures
• International judicial proceedings
③ Provided data shall not be used in a manner that disadvantages the contributor’s competitive environment.
④ This Article shall be interpreted as a protective standard intended to ensure the sustainability of private-sector participation.
