1-7. Responsibility Attribution & Allocation Framework

Submitted by: Jungyun Kwon, Citizen of the Republic of Korea and Global Citizen
Date: March 19, 2026 (KST)

Article 1. Purpose
① This regulation establishes the criteria and procedures for attributing responsibility for acts of aggression and allocating corresponding shares of responsibility.
② The purposes of this regulation are as follows:
• Fair allocation of responsibility for aggression
• Analysis of multi-actor structures
• Providing a basis for reparations and sanctions measures
• Supporting international judicial proceedings
③ This regulation is linked to the following documents:
• Definition of Aggression
• Actor Identification & Attribution Framework
• Aggression Determination Framework
④ This regulation operates as an analytical and allocation-guidance framework, not as a mechanism for final determination of responsibility.
Article 2. Principles of Responsibility Attribution
① Attribution of responsibility shall be conducted in accordance with the following principles:
• Evidence-based principle
• Principle of proportional responsibility
• Principle of causation
• Principle of consistency with international law
② Responsibility shall be assessed by comprehensively considering the actor’s conduct, command role, and level of support.
③ Allocation of responsibility may apply to both single-actor and multi-actor structures.
④ Responsibility assessments shall remain aligned with the findings of the Actor Identification & Attribution Framework.
Article 3. Categories of Responsibility
① Responsibility in aggression-related incidents may be classified into the following categories:
• Primary Responsibility
• Direct execution of the attack
• Conduct of military operations
• Command Responsibility
• Operational command
• Issuance of attack orders
• Operational Responsibility
• Operational planning
• Support for military action
• Material Support Responsibility
• Provision of weapons
• Provision of intelligence
• Logistical support
• Facilitation Responsibility
• Provision of attack infrastructure
• Provision of cyber infrastructure
② Categories of responsibility may be applied cumulatively.
Article 4. Responsibility Factors
① Allocation of responsibility shares shall consider the following factors:
• Degree of direct attack execution
• Level of command and control
• Participation in preparation and planning
• Level of support for execution
• Degree of influence on the outcome of the attack
② Each factor may be assessed through quantitative or qualitative analysis.
③ Assessment criteria shall be applied in a reproducible and comparable manner.
Article 5. Responsibility Allocation Model
① Responsibility shares may be determined through the following models:
• Direct-actor-centered model
• Command-structure-centered model
• Contribution-based model
② Responsibility shall be allocated on the basis of 100% of total responsibility.
③ In the case of a single actor, full responsibility shall be attributed to that actor.
④ Responsibility allocation methods may be applied in combination depending on the characteristics of the incident.
Article 6. Multi-Actor Responsibility
① Where multiple actors are involved, responsibility shall be distributed according to the following forms:
• Responsibility for execution of the attack
• Command responsibility
• Support responsibility
② Each actor’s share shall be determined in proportion to its contribution.
③ A responsibility structure may be expressed as follows:
• Primary Actor → 60%
• Command Actor → 25%
• Support Actor → 15%
④ In a multi-actor structure, where a particular actor lacks the capacity to provide reparations or fails to discharge responsibility, the following principles may apply:
• Joint and Several Liability, whereby command actors or principal supporting actors cover the shortfall
• Reallocation of responsibility shares in cases of evasion of responsibility or concealment of assets
⑤ Application of joint and several liability shall consider the following factors:
• Level of command and control
• Structural influence
• Degree of control over funds and assets
⑥ Application of joint and several liability shall remain aligned with the State Responsibility & Reparations Framework.
⑦ Responsibility of Participation Actors in aggression shall be assessed in accordance with the following criteria:
• Contribution to execution of aggression
• Nature and impact of the supporting conduct
• Causal contribution to the operational outcome
⑧ Participation Actors may be classified under the following categories of responsibility:
• Material Support Responsibility
• Facilitation Responsibility
• Operational Support Responsibility
⑨ Participation Actors shall be included in the allocation of responsibility shares according to their contribution, and in certain cases may be assigned a substantial share of responsibility.
⑩ Where the following conditions are met, claims for reparations for harm may be limited or excluded with respect to a Participation Actor or the relevant state:
• Existence of substantial contribution to the act of aggression
• The harm occurred in the course of response to aggression
⑪ In such cases, the relevant actor may be regarded as engaging in Self-Risk Participation.
⑫ However, exceptions may apply where the following are established:
• Clear defensive purpose
• Absence of causal linkage to the act of aggression
Article 7. State Responsibility Allocation
① Where a state is involved in an act of aggression, responsibility shall be assessed according to the following criteria:
• Direct participation of state armed forces
• State direction or control
• State provision of material or technical support
② State responsibility shall be assessed in accordance with principles of international law.
③ State responsibility may serve as a basis for state reparations and sanctions determinations.
④ Determination of state responsibility shall remain aligned with the State Responsibility Differentiation & Attribution Framework.
Article 8. Responsibility Report
① Results of responsibility analysis shall be prepared in the form of a Responsibility Attribution Report.
② The report shall include the following:
• Incident overview
• Analysis of actor structure
• Analysis of categories of responsibility
• Allocation of responsibility shares
• List of evidence
③ The report shall be submitted to the aggression determination authority.
④ The report may be linked to the Responsibility Dossier Generation System.
Article 9. Judicial and Financial Application
① Responsibility shares may be used in the following procedures:
• International judicial proceedings
• Reparations negotiations
• Sanctions measures
② Reports may be transmitted to the following institutions or jurisdictions:
• Domestic judicial authorities
• International Criminal Court (ICC)
• International Court of Justice (ICJ)
• Other competent courts or prosecutorial authorities
③ Responsibility shares may be used as a basis for state reparations and international financial sanctions.
④ Submission procedures shall remain aligned with the Multi-Jurisdiction Judicial Submission Protocol.
⑤ Financial application shall remain aligned with the following documents:
• Actor-Based Financial Risk Flagging Framework
• Conditional Asset Restriction Protocol
• Victim Impact & Damage Documentation Framework
Article 10. Transparency and Review
① A certain level of disclosure may be made in order to ensure the credibility of responsibility analysis procedures.
② Disclosable content may include:
• Responsibility structure
• Analytical methods
• Responsibility shares
③ Where new evidence is discovered, responsibility analysis may be subject to review.
④ Disclosure shall be conducted with due regard to the principles of civilian protection and prevention of misuse of information.
Article 11. Relationship with the Global Collective Defense System
① This regulation operates as the responsibility analysis framework of the Global Collective Defense System.
② This regulation is linked to the following documents:
• Actor Identification & Attribution Framework
• Aggression Determination Framework
• Collective Response Framework
③ This regulation is responsible for the allocation of responsibility and linkage to execution within the system.
Article 12. Mitigating Factors
① Responsibility may be mitigated where the following conditions are met:
• Participation under coercion or threat
• Immediate cessation or cooperation upon recognizing aggression
• Provision of internal information
• Blocking of attack infrastructure
• Cooperation with international investigations
② Whether mitigation applies shall consider the following factors:
• Actual level of influence
• Role performed
• Contribution to reduction of harm
③ Mitigation shall be applied through adjustment of responsibility shares without altering the overall structure.
④ Application of mitigation shall be limited to adjustments of allocation shares without distorting the responsibility structure.
⑤ Where the following conditions are met, aggression-related participation may be assessed as Involuntary or Constrained Participation:
• External military threats or coercion
• Structural constraints arising from existing military alliances or stationing agreements
• Limitation of genuine decision-making autonomy
• Immediate refusal would pose a significant risk to state survival or public safety
⑥ Where such conditions are recognized, responsibility of the relevant actor may be adjusted as follows:
• Partial reduction of responsibility share
• Reclassification of responsibility type (Primary → Supporting / Facilitation)
• Adjustment of sanctions or reparations contribution level
⑦ Additional factors to be considered in mitigation include:
• Existence of actual choice
• Intensity and duration of the pressure
• Expected harm resulting from refusal
• Cooperation after participation or efforts to minimize harm
⑧ Mitigation may be restricted where the following conduct is identified:
• Voluntary expansion of participation
• Provision of additional support
• Active cooperation in acts of aggression
⑨ This Article does not imply:
• Automatic exemption from responsibility
• Complete exclusion from the responsibility structure
⑩ Whether mitigation applies and its extent shall be specified in the Responsibility Attribution Report.
Article 13. Cooperation Incentives
① Incentives may be considered where cooperation occurs, including:
• Provision of internal documents
• Disclosure of operational information
• Cooperation in freezing assets
• Cooperation in harm recovery
② Incentives may be reflected in the following procedures:
• Adjustment of responsibility shares
• Consideration of mitigation of sanctions
• Reparations negotiations
③ Cooperation does not imply immunity from responsibility.
④ Cooperative information must satisfy the following conditions:
• Consistency with existing monitoring data
• Passage of technical verification procedures
• Completion of multi-source cross-verification
⑤ Unverified information shall not be used in incentive determinations.
⑥ This Article shall remain aligned with the Cooperative Disclosure & Conditional Immunity Protocol.
Article 14. Corporate and Non-State Responsibility
① Responsibility may apply not only to states but also to private organizations.
② Relevant entities may include the following:
• Private military companies
• Weapons manufacturing companies
• Military technology companies
• Cyber attack organizations
• Infrastructure-providing enterprises
③ Responsibility may be recognized under the following conditions:
• Knowledge of aggression
• Direct support
• Provision of infrastructure
• Participation in operations
④ The following actors may also be subject to attribution of responsibility:
• Parent or controlling companies
• Major shareholders exercising effective control
• Entities controlling decision-making through governance structures
⑤ Corporate responsibility shall be assessed under the following categories:
• Material Support Responsibility
• Facilitation Responsibility
⑥ This Article shall be interpreted as a standard intended to clarify the responsibility structure of non-state actors.
Article 15. Illicit Corporate Participation
① The following conduct may be considered unlawful participation:
• Supplying weapons for the purpose of aggression
• Providing attack technologies
• Providing cyber infrastructure
• Concluding operational support contracts
② Such conduct may be assessed under the following categories of responsibility:
• Operational Responsibility
• Material Support Responsibility
• Facilitation Responsibility
③ Responsibility shall be assessed in accordance with international law.
④ Such responsibility may be linked to international judicial proceedings and financial sanctions.
Article 16. Financial and Asset Liability
① The following measures may apply to actors whose responsibility has been established:
• Reparations liability
• Asset freezing
• International financial sanctions
② Responsibility shares may be used for the following purposes:
• Allocation of reparations burdens
• Contributions to international funds
• Asset confiscation and recovery
③ Such measures may be implemented through international cooperation or judicial procedures.
④ This Article shall remain aligned with the following documents:
• State Responsibility & Reparations Framework
• Judicial Execution & Asset Recovery Protocol
