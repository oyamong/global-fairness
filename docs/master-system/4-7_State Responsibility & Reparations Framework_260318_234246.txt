4-7. State Responsibility & Reparations Framework

Submitted by: Jungyun Kwon, Citizen of the Republic of Korea and Global Citizen
Date: March 19, 2026 (KST)

Article 1. Purpose
① This framework defines a state responsibility-based reparations and recovery system for harm arising from acts of aggression and related conduct.
② The purposes are as follows:
• Ensuring meaningful reparations for victims
• Establishing a structural linkage between state responsibility and reparations
• Recovering and redistributing gains derived from unlawful conduct
• Advancing international justice and restorative justice
Article 2. Fundamental Principle
① Reparations shall follow the following principles:
• Responsibility-based reparations principle
• Victim-centered principle
• Principle of proportionality
• Principle of effective restoration
② State reparations shall be applied on the following premises:
• Only where state responsibility has been established
• Without replacing individual responsibility
③ Reparations shall include not only monetary payment but also restorative justice.
Article 3. Scope of State Reparations
① State reparations may include:
• Reparations for direct harm
• Compensation for indirect and structural harm
• Support for recovery from long-term harm
② The following may be included:
• Individual victims
• Groups and communities
• Injured states or non-state protected entities
③ In the case of non-state protected entities, inclusion may apply where the following conditions are met:
• Substantial harm has occurred
• The entity may be recognized as a protected group
• It is consistent with international human rights standards
Article 4. Types of Reparations
① Reparations may take the following forms:
1. Restitution
• Return of property
• Restoration of rights
• Restoration of prior status
2. Compensation
• Compensation for property loss
• Compensation for loss of income
• Compensation for economic harm
3. Rehabilitation
• Medical assistance
• Psychological recovery
• Support for social reintegration
4. Satisfaction
• Official acknowledgment and apology
• Factual record
• Recognition of harm
5. Guarantees of Non-Repetition
• Institutional improvement
• Structural reform
• Strengthened monitoring and controls
Article 5. Attribution of Reparations
① Reparative responsibility shall be determined according to the following criteria:
• State Responsibility Level
• Contribution to harm
• Continuity and impact of the conduct
② Reparations ratios shall be linked to:
• Responsibility Dossier
• Victim Impact Documentation
• Asset Recovery outcomes
③ Where the following conditions are met, the relevant harm may be evaluated as Self-Risk Participation Damage arising from participation within an aggression structure:
• The harm is causally linked to Aggression-Related Participation
• The harm arose during defensive or responsive action to aggression
④ In such cases, the harm may be treated as follows:
• Excluded from ordinary reparations eligibility, or
• Assessed as eligible only for limited reparations
⑤ Such determinations shall be governed by:
• Victim Impact & Damage Documentation Framework
• Responsibility Attribution & Allocation Framework
• State Responsibility Differentiation & Attribution Framework
Article 6. Funding Sources
① Reparations funds may be composed of the following:
• Recovered illicit assets
• Compensation payments based on state responsibility
• International cooperative funds
• Legal sanctions and fines
② Funding may operate through a mixed structure.
③ Recovered assets shall be prioritized for use as reparations resources.
Article 7. Escrow & Holding Mechanism
① Assets related to reparations may be managed through the following structures:
• Escrow accounts held by internationally trusted institutions
• Neutral management systems
② Such assets may be converted into reparations upon satisfaction of the following conditions:
• Final legal determination
• Finalized responsibility structure
• Resolution of disputes
③ Assets subject to dispute shall be preserved pending resolution.
Article 8. Distribution Mechanism
① Reparations shall be distributed according to the following criteria:
• Scale of harm
• Type of harm
• Duration of harm
• Responsibility ratio
② The following distribution methods may be applied:
• Direct payment
• Collective compensation funds
• Recovery and reconstruction projects
③ The following principles shall apply:
• Prevention of duplicate reparations
• Fair distribution
• Transparency
Article 9. Provisional Compensation Mechanism
① Provisional compensation may be provided where the following conditions are met:
• The scale of harm is clearly identified
• There is an urgent need for restoration
• Recovery of assets or final responsibility determination is expected to be delayed
② Provisional compensation may be funded through:
• A portion of recovered assets
• International cooperative funds
• Conditional state contributions
③ Provisional compensation shall later be settled through:
• Finalization of responsibility ratios
• Reallocation of costs among states and actors
④ Provisional compensation shall follow these principles:
• Victim protection takes priority
• Prevention of overpayment
• Transparent settlement structure
Article 10. Residual Liability & State Guarantee Mechanism
① Where total harm is not fully covered by recovered assets and individual responsibility, the state may bear supplementary reparative responsibility.
② The following may apply according to the state’s level of responsibility:
• Level 1 → Full guarantee
• Level 2 → Proportional guarantee
• Level 3 → Limited guarantee
③ State guarantees may cover:
• Unpaid reparations
• Long-term recovery costs
• Restoration of public infrastructure
④ After providing such guarantees, the state may exercise the following rights:
• Right of recourse against responsible actors
• Participation in recovery proceedings
Article 11. Multi-Jurisdiction Coordination
① Reparations may be carried out within a multi-jurisdictional environment.
② The following principles shall apply:
• Inter-jurisdictional cooperation
• Priority to international standards
• Consideration of the place where the harm occurred
③ Through international cooperation, the following may be carried out:
• Asset securing
• Reparations execution
• Information sharing
Article 12. Retroactive & Continuing Harm Application
① Retroactive application may be possible in the following cases:
• Grave acts of aggression
• Continuing harm that persists to the present
② Even in past cases, this framework may apply where the following conditions are met:
• Continuing harm exists
• The responsibility structure can be identified
• Evidence can be secured
③ This provision is intended to prevent evasion of responsibility through the passage of time.
Article 13. Non-Compliance & Enforcement Linkage
① Where a state fails to comply with reparations obligations, the following may apply:
• Restriction of financial network access
• Asset tracing and additional recovery
• Linkage to international cooperative sanctions
② The following measures may be applied progressively:
• Level 1: Warning and consultation
• Level 2: Limited financial measures
• Level 3: Expanded network sanctions
③ These measures shall be linked to:
• Financial Risk Flag Framework
• Asset Recovery Protocol
Article 14. Safeguards & Fairness
① The following principles shall apply:
• Prohibition of excessive reparations
• Protection of innocent states and groups
• Maintenance of proportionality
② The following shall be ensured:
• Consideration of exculpatory evidence
• Reflection of mitigating factors
• Application of objective criteria
③ Where the following conditions are proven, harm suffered by a participating state or participating actor may exceptionally be included within the scope of reparations:
• Involuntary or coerced participation
• Absence of meaningful decision-making autonomy
• Rejection of participation would have posed a grave threat to the survival of the state
• Active efforts were undertaken to minimize harm
④ In such cases, the following may apply:
• Recognition of partial reparations
• Reparations in the form of supplementary or humanitarian support
• Limited application of reparations ratios
⑤ Such determinations shall follow the following principles:
• Principle of proportionality
• Fact-based assessment
• Consistency with international law
⑥ This provision does not mean:
• Automatic recognition of reparations
• Complete exemption from the responsibility structure
Article 15. Right to Clarification
① The state concerned shall have the right to:
• Challenge the scope of responsibility
• Request adjustment of reparations amount
• Rebut evidence
② The results of clarification shall be reflected in the reparations structure.
Article 16. Human Final Authority
① Reparations decisions shall be subject to human review.
② Human decision-makers shall have the authority to:
• Approve or adjust reparations
• Determine the method of distribution
• Order emergency suspension
Article 17. Transparency & Auditability
① All reparations processes shall be recorded.
② Records shall include:
• Basis for reparations
• Sources of funds
• Distribution details
• Review process
③ Such records shall be used for ex post audit and legal verification.
Article 18. System Integration & Reparative Coordination
① This framework is fully integrated with the responsibility, harm, asset, and enforcement systems within the Global Collective Defense System.
② Reparative responsibility and the scope of reparations shall remain consistent with:
• State Responsibility Differentiation & Attribution Framework
• Responsibility Attribution & Allocation Framework
• Responsibility Dossier Generation System
③ The scale of harm, structure of harm, and reparations priorities shall be calculated on the basis of:
• Victim Impact & Damage Documentation Framework
④ Recovered assets, state guarantees, and execution procedures shall be linked to:
• Judicial Execution & Asset Recovery Protocol
⑤ Determinations regarding continuing harm and the present relevance of past cases shall remain consistent with:
• Temporal Applicability & Continuing Harm Framework
⑥ Reparations-related materials may, where necessary, be submitted to domestic and international judicial authorities through:
• Multi-Jurisdiction Judicial Submission Protocol
⑦ This framework functions as the system’s Final Reparative Layer, comprehensively integrating responsibility, harm, assets, and enforcement outcomes into a structure of restorative justice and reparations.
