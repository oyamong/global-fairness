1-1. Definition of Aggression

Submitted by: Jungyun Kwon, Citizen of the Republic of Korea and Global Citizen
Date: March 19, 2026 (KST)

Article 1. Purpose
① This document establishes the definition and fundamental principles of Aggression in order to protect international peace, state sovereignty, and the survival foundations of populations.
② This definition serves the following purposes:
• Clear identification of acts of aggression
• Provision of a basis for collective international response
• Strengthening deterrence against aggression
• Supporting international responsibility and legal processes
③ This definition maintains consistency with the following international norms:
• United Nations Charter
• United Nations General Assembly Resolution 3314
④ This definition operates as a non-punitive, normative framework, and final determinations and legal responsibility shall be established through separate judicial processes and relevant frameworks.
Article 2. Core Definition of Aggression
① Aggression refers to acts committed by a state, or by actors under its effective control, direction, or support, against another state or a Protected Entity as defined in Article 3, for the purpose of violating:
• Territory
• Sovereignty or governing functions
• The survival foundations of a population
② Aggression includes the use of force or coercive measures equivalent to force.
③ Aggression is established when one or more of the following domains are violated:
• Territorial integrity
• Sovereignty
• Survival foundations of a population
④ This definition shall be interpreted based on the impact and results, rather than the form of the act.
Article 3. Protected Entities
① The targets of aggression under this definition are not limited to states.
② The following shall be included as Protected Entities:
• Sovereign states
• Partially recognized states
• Territories with incomplete status under international law
• Territories with de facto governing structures
• Occupied territories
• Distinct populations or communities
③ An entity shall qualify as a Protected Entity if it meets the following conditions:
• Presence of a continuous population
• Existence of survival foundations
• Existence of a minimum level of social or administrative structure
④ This Article does not alter or affect the recognition of statehood under international law.
⑤ The interpretative principle of this Article is as follows:
“The determining factor is not statehood, but the existence of a survival structure requiring protection.”
Article 4. Coercive Measures Equivalent to Force
① The following acts may be considered coercive measures equivalent to force:
• Cyber attacks disabling critical national infrastructure
• Attacks collapsing national financial systems
• Intentional disruption of energy supply chains
• Attacks targeting food supply systems
• Large-scale economic or logistical restriction measures
② Such acts may be considered aggression when the following conditions are met:
• Substantial paralysis of state functions
• Significant impact on the survival foundations of the population
③ Criteria for assessing “large-scale economic restriction” include:
• Scale of damage relative to GDP
• Level of disruption to critical supply chains
• Impairment of financial system functionality
• Impact on survival foundations
④ Detailed criteria are delegated to:
• Aggression Determination Framework
Article 5. Territorial Aggression
① Territorial aggression refers to physical or military violations of territorial control.
② Representative acts include:
• Intrusion across borders or lines of effective control
• Occupation of territory or islands
• Amphibious military landings
• Airspace intrusion and attack
• Control of ports and coastlines
• Forced control over maritime domains
③ Such acts may be considered aggression.
Article 6. Sovereignty Aggression
① Sovereignty aggression refers to the substantial neutralization of political independence or governing functions.
② Included acts:
• Military actions aimed at overthrowing a government
• Attacks on command and control systems
• Attacks on military installations
• Large-scale cyber attacks
• Neutralization of satellite and communication infrastructure
③ Sustained disruption of governing functions may be considered aggression.
Article 7. Survival Aggression
① Survival aggression refers to acts targeting the life-sustaining foundations of populations.
② The following acts may fall under this category:
• Attacks on power grids
• Destruction of water infrastructure
• Attacks on food supply chains
• Attacks on hospitals and medical facilities
• Disruption of energy supply
• Large-scale environmental destruction
③ Intentional targeting of survival foundations may be considered aggression.
Article 8. Gray Zone & Proxy Aggression Threshold
① The following may constitute gray zone or proxy-based aggression:
• Private Military Companies (PMCs)
• Proxy armed groups
• Unofficial cyber organizations
② Attribution of responsibility shall consider the following factors:
• Level of control
• Knowledge or constructive knowledge
• Support or safe haven
• Failure to prevent
• Pattern and persistence
③ Where these factors meet a certain threshold, the conduct may be assessed as indirect aggression or attributable aggression.
④ Specific thresholds are delegated to:
• Actor Identification & Attribution Framework
• State Responsibility Differentiation & Attribution Framework
Article 9. Modern Forms of Aggression
① Aggression may also occur through modern means such as:
• AI-enabled and autonomous weapon attacks
• Autonomous drone attacks
• Unmanned combat system operations
• AI-based automated strike systems
• Cyber attacks
• Critical infrastructure disruption
• Financial and administrative system neutralization
• Attacks on core data infrastructure
• Space-based attacks
• Satellite destruction
• Disruption of satellite communications or GPS
• Subsea infrastructure attacks
• Destruction of submarine communication cables
• Attacks on subsea energy pipelines
② Even where attribution is unclear, a situation may be classified as a Provisional Aggression Status if:
• The scale of damage reaches the functional level of a state or Protected Entity
• The attack is organized and sustained
• Multiple critical infrastructures are affected simultaneously or sequentially
③ Provisional Aggression Status indicates:
• Not a final determination of aggression
• A state of limited response readiness
• Ongoing verification procedures
④ Final determination shall follow:
• Aggression Determination Framework
Article 10. State Responsibility
① Responsibility for acts of aggression may arise where:
• The act is carried out directly by a state
• The act is carried out by entities under the state’s effective control
• The state supports or facilitates the act
② Where a state has the capacity to prevent an act but intentionally fails to do so, a degree of responsibility may be recognized.
③ Assessment of responsibility levels shall be aligned with:
• Responsibility Attribution & Allocation Framework
• State Responsibility Differentiation & Attribution Framework
Article 11. Non-State Actors
① The following actors are classified as non-state actors:
• Terrorist organizations
• Militias and paramilitary groups
• Mercenary groups
• Cyber attack organizations
• Civilian entities with the capability to attack critical infrastructure
② Such conduct may be considered aggression if:
• Conducted under effective state control or direction
• Conducted with state support or protection
• The state intentionally fails to prevent the conduct
③ Even in the absence of state control, collective international response may be initiated where:
• Damage reaches the level of a state or Protected Entity
• The attack is organized and sustained
• There is significant impact on survival foundations
④ Relevant procedures shall follow:
• Aggression Determination Framework
• Actor Identification & Attribution Framework
Article 12. Determination of Aggression
① Determination of aggression shall consider:
• Use of force or coercive measures
• Scale and duration of the attack
• State responsibility or control
• Scope of the affected target
② Formal determination shall follow:
• Aggression Determination Framework
Article 13. Relationship with Self-Defense
① In the event of aggression, states and collective defense systems may exercise the right of self-defense.
② Exercise of self-defense shall comply with:
• Necessity
• Proportionality
• Protection of civilians
③ The consequences of defensive actions shall, in principle, fall within the responsibility of the aggressor state.
④ Provided that:
• Efforts are made to comply with international humanitarian law
• Measures are taken to minimize civilian harm
• Necessity and proportionality are maintained
⑤ Where these conditions are not met, responsibility may be assessed separately.
⑥ Where the following conditions are met, damage causally linked to such participation may be considered as arising from the exercise of self-defense:
• The actor or state is engaged in Aggression-Related Participation
⑦ In such cases, the actor or state may be regarded as part of the participation structure defined in Article 14.
⑧ Responsibility and reparations shall be assessed in accordance with:
• Responsibility Attribution & Allocation Framework
• State Responsibility Differentiation & Attribution Framework
⑨ This Article shall be applied in accordance with:
• Principle of necessity
• Principle of proportionality
• Principle of civilian protection
Article 14. Aggression-Related Participation
① The following acts do not constitute aggression in themselves but may be assessed as Aggression-Related Participation, enabling the execution of aggression.
② Such acts may include:
• Support for military operations
• Provision of weapons or military supplies
• Financial or funding support
• Intelligence and reconnaissance support
• Cyber operational support
• Provision of territory, bases, or military infrastructure
③ Such acts may be considered part of the aggression structure where a substantial linkage to acts of aggression is established.
④ Attribution and responsibility shall be determined in accordance with:
• Actor Identification & Attribution Framework
• Responsibility Attribution & Allocation Framework
• State Responsibility Differentiation & Attribution Framework
⑤ Aggression-related participation may include the actor or state within a Participation Structure of aggression.
⑥ The status and level of responsibility shall be assessed in accordance with the above frameworks.
⑦ This Article does not automatically classify a state as an aggressor state.
⑧ Where the following conditions exist, such participation may be considered as mitigating or adjusting factors:
• External military pressure or coercion
• Constraints arising from existing defense or stationing agreements
• Limited decision-making autonomy
• Immediate refusal posing significant risk to state survival
⑨ These conditions do not imply:
• Automatic exemption from responsibility
• Complete exclusion from the responsibility structure
⑩ Responsibility and reparations shall be assessed in accordance with:
• Responsibility Attribution & Allocation Framework
• State Responsibility Differentiation & Attribution Framework
⑪ This Article shall be applied in accordance with:
• Principle of proportionality
• Fact-based assessment
• Consistency with international law
Article 15. Role in the Global Collective Defense System
① This definition functions as a foundational normative standard within the Global Collective Defense System.
② This definition is structurally linked to the following documents:
• Aggression Determination Framework
• Actor Identification & Attribution Framework
• Responsibility Attribution & Allocation Framework
• Victim Impact & Damage Documentation Framework
• State Responsibility & Reparations Framework
③ This definition serves as the reference baseline for all determinations and procedures within the system.
