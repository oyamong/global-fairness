Global Collective Defense System
PHASE 1: Operational Detection & Response Infrastructure
4-6. Victim Impact & Damage Documentation Framework

Submitted by: Jungyun Kwon, Citizen of the Republic of Korea and Global Citizen
Date: March 19, 2026 (KST)

Article 1. Purpose
① This framework defines the standards for systematically recording, analyzing, and structuring Victim Impact and Damage resulting from acts of aggression and related activities.
② The purposes are as follows:
• Visualization and quantification of harm
• Establishment of linkage between responsibility and damage
• Provision of a foundation for judicial proceedings and reparations discussions
③ This framework includes:
• Provision of baseline data for reparations calculations
• Support for designing compensation structures based on individual and state responsibility
Article 2. Definition of Victim & Damage
① Victims include:
• Individuals
• Groups
• Communities
• States
• Protected Entities
• Populations and collectives
② Damage includes the following categories:
• Physical damage
• Human casualties
• Economic damage
• Social damage
• Long-term structural damage
③ The definition of damage is directly linked to the scope of reparations.
④ Damage may be classified according to its origin structure as:
• Direct Victim Impact
• Indirect Victim Impact
• Structural Participation Impact
⑤ Structural Participation Impact refers to harm linked to participation within an aggression structure, and its eligibility for reparations shall be determined under separate responsibility and reparations frameworks.
Article 3. Scope of Documentation
① Documentation shall include:
• Direct damage
• Indirect damage
• Cascading damage
• Potential damage
② Both short-term and medium- to long-term impacts shall be included.
③ The scope of documentation shall serve as a basis for determining the extent of reparations liability.
Article 4. Damage Classification
① Damage shall be classified as follows:
1. Human Impact
• Death
• Injury
• Health effects
2. Economic Damage
• Property loss
• Income reduction
• Industrial disruption
3. Social Impact
• Community breakdown
• Social instability
• Disruption of services
4. Infrastructure Damage
• Transportation
• Energy
• Communication
• Public facilities
5. Long-Term Structural Impact
• Educational disruption
• Intergenerational effects
• Regional economic collapse
② Each damage category may be assigned separate coefficients or weightings in reparations calculations.
Article 5. Data Collection Standards
① Damage data shall adhere to the following standards:
• Multi-source verification
• Integration of direct and indirect data
• Time-based recording
• Continuous updates
② At least two independent data sources shall be required.
③ The following data may be used as supplementary sources:
• Satellite and remote sensing data
• Financial and economic indicators
• Communication and infrastructure disruption records
• Reports from international organizations and NGOs
④ Collected data shall be linked to reparations calculation systems.
Article 6. Quantification & Measurement
① Damage shall be quantified wherever possible:
• Monetary valuation
• Population-based impact
• Duration-based effects
② Where quantification is not feasible, qualitative analysis shall be applied.
③ The following standards may be used:
• Reference to international reparations standards
• Comparative analysis across similar damage types
• Long-term loss estimation models
④ Quantification results shall be directly used for:
• Determining compensation amounts
• Applying responsibility ratios
• Establishing supplementary state compensation
Article 7. Victim Mapping
① Victims shall be mapped within the network structure.
② The following linkages shall be analyzed:
• Actor-to-victim relationships
• Network-based propagation of harm
• Asset flow-to-impact relationships
③ Damage shall be analyzed as a structural network phenomenon, not as isolated incidents.
④ Analysis may include:
• Identification of high-impact regions
• Assessment of vulnerable populations
• Secondary harm propagation pathways
⑤ Victim mapping may include relationships among:
• States
• Protected Entities
• Populations and communities
⑥ Mapping results shall directly inform reparations distribution structures.
Article 8. Linkage to Responsibility
① All documented damage shall be linked to:
• Responsibility Dossiers
• Actor Identification systems
• Network Analysis systems
② Each instance of damage shall be attributable to specific actors or structures.
③ Linkage criteria shall include:
• Direct causation
• Indirect influence
• Structural contribution
④ The following subjects may be considered in attribution:
• States
• Protected Entities
• Non-state actors
• Network-based operational structures
⑤ Attribution results shall determine:
• Individual responsibility scope
• State supplementary responsibility
• Allocation ratios for reparations
⑥ Damage may be classified as Self-Risk Participation Impact when:
• It is causally linked to participation in aggression-related activities
• It arises during response or defensive actions
⑦ In such cases, damage may be evaluated as:
• Structurally distinct from general victim impact
• Subject to limited consideration for reparations
⑧ Applicability of reparations in such cases shall follow:
• Responsibility Attribution & Allocation Framework
• State Responsibility Differentiation & Attribution Framework
• State Responsibility & Reparations Framework
⑨ Exceptions may apply where:
• Participation was involuntary or coerced
• No meaningful choice existed
• Active measures were taken to minimize harm
⑩ This provision does not imply:
• Exclusion of damage records
• Denial of harm itself
Article 9. Temporal Analysis
① Damage shall be analyzed over time:
• Point of occurrence
• Expansion trajectory
• Duration
② Patterns of harm propagation shall be recorded.
③ Analysis may include:
• Rate of escalation
• Factors delaying recovery
• Long-term cumulative effects
④ Long-term damage may receive additional weighting in reparations calculations.
Article 10. Dynamic Update Mechanism
① Damage records shall be continuously updated:
• Emergence of new damage
• Expansion of existing damage
• Incorporation of recovery status
② All updates shall be recorded.
③ The following status categories may be applied:
• Ongoing
• Stabilizing
• Recovered
④ Updated data may be used for recalculating reparations.
Article 11. Safeguards & Ethical Standards
① The following principles shall apply:
• Protection of victim privacy
• Prevention of secondary harm
• Protection of sensitive information
② Documentation shall comply with human rights standards.
③ Protective measures may include:
• Anonymization and de-identification
• Restricted access to sensitive data
• Disclosure based on victim consent
④ The same ethical standards shall apply during reparations processes.
Article 12. Right to Clarification
① Stakeholders related to damage records shall have the right to:
• Request correction of errors
• Request modification of damage scope
• Submit additional information
② Records may be revised upon review.
③ The correction process shall be recorded and managed transparently.
④ Corrections shall be reflected in reparations calculations.
Article 13. Integration with Legal Evidence
① Damage records shall be directly linked to:
• Legal Evidence Packages
• Responsibility Dossiers
• Asset Analysis systems
② Records shall be maintained in a form suitable for judicial use.
③ They may be used for:
• Damage assessment
• Responsibility allocation analysis
• Judicial support materials
④ This framework shall maintain consistency with:
• Definition of Aggression frameworks
• Protected Entities-based damage structures
• Multi-Jurisdiction Judicial Submission systems
⑤ This framework shall be directly linked to reparations execution protocols.
Article 14. Transparency & Auditability
① All damage documentation processes shall be recorded.
② Records shall include:
• Data sources
• Analytical processes
• Modification history
③ Records shall be used for post hoc verification and audit.
④ Reparations calculation and distribution criteria shall also be recorded and subject to audit.
Article 15. System Integration & Reparations Linkage
① This framework is fully integrated with all accountability, evidence, and asset analysis systems within the Global Collective Defense System.
② Damage documentation and analysis shall remain aligned with:
• Actor Identification & Attribution Framework
• Responsibility Attribution & Allocation Framework
• Responsibility Dossier Generation System
③ Damage data shall be combined with asset and financial data from:
• Influence-Linked Asset Monitoring Framework
• Financial Network Alert & Integration Protocol
④ Damage and loss data generated by this framework shall be utilized as legal evidence through:
• Legal Evidence Packaging Protocol
• Chain of Custody & Evidence Integrity Framework
⑤ Analytical results may be submitted to judicial and relevant authorities through:
• Multi-Jurisdiction Judicial Submission Protocol
⑥ This framework shall be directly linked to:
• State Responsibility & Reparations Framework
• State Responsibility Differentiation & Attribution Framework
⑦ This framework functions as the system’s
Core Damage-to-Reparation Layer, transforming structured harm into accountable and enforceable reparations mechanisms.
