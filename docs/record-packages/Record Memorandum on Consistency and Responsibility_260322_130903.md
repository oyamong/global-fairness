1. Purpose of the Document
This document has been prepared to objectively record and preserve a structured review of the recently issued Joint Statement concerning the Strait of Hormuz, focusing on its consistency, scope of application, and the extent to which responsibility structures are reflected.
The purpose of this document is to preserve a record of consistency and responsibility considerations based on international principles, to examine whether relevant standards have been applied consistently or selectively, to document responsibility structures among actors involved in the conflict, and to establish a foundational record that may be referenced in future institutional reviews, audits, or judicial proceedings.
This document is prepared from the perspective of a global citizen and does not represent any specific state, government, or political interest. It is not intended to advocate political claims or diplomatic positions, but rather constitutes an objective record based on publicly available information and international principles, focusing on consistency, normative completeness, and the preservation of responsibility structures.
This document represents a record prepared as of the time of writing, based on publicly available information and international principles, and may be subject to revision should additional factual information become available.

4. Factual Structure
The situation under review appears to involve inter-state military tensions and escalation, threats to maritime transportation and energy infrastructure, restrictions or threats to navigation in the Strait of Hormuz, increased risks or damage to civilian vessels and energy-related facilities, and the issuance of an international Joint Statement in response.

5. Consistency and Responsibility Review
This document reviews the matter across four principal dimensions: use of force, self-defense, maritime law, and civilian protection. Each dimension is considered in light of potential violations and justifications under international law, with the review grounded not in political preference but in the internal coherence and scope of application of the relevant framework.

6. Consistency Analysis
The Joint Statement reflects concern regarding certain international legal principles. However, its treatment of the overall structure of the conflict appears limited. In particular, the review of prior uses of force, the broader escalation structure, the interaction among multiple actors involved in the conflict, and the overall allocation of responsibility across those actors do not appear to be sufficiently addressed.
Furthermore, while the statement emphasizes the consequences and risks associated with the blockade or threat to navigation in the Strait of Hormuz, the background and underlying causes leading to such circumstances, the formation process of the blockade risk, and the extent to which participating states undertook diplomatic efforts or fact-finding measures to mitigate or clarify these causes are not sufficiently reflected within the text. As a result, the statement presents an emphasis on outcomes while leaving the underlying causal structure and records of mediation or mitigation efforts relatively limited.
These limitations do not in themselves negate the value of the statement. However, in order to substantially enhance the credibility and persuasiveness of its international legal message, a more complete and balanced review encompassing the causes, processes, and outcomes of the conflict would be necessary. Only with such supplementation can the statement be understood not merely as a selective response to particular outcomes, but as a consistent and reliable expression of international legal standards.

7. Responsibility Gap Analysis
While the statement presents concerns and observations regarding specific acts, it does not appear to sufficiently address the comprehensive responsibility structure involving the actor or actors responsible for the initial escalation of force, those who materially contributed to the expansion of the conflict, and the broader set of actors influencing the current situation.
As a result, certain elements of responsibility remain insufficiently recorded within the text of the statement. From a consistency perspective, as distinct from diplomatic considerations, there appear to have been opportunities for a more comprehensive articulation of responsibility structures that may have held both structural and institutional significance. In this context, it may be observed that caution appears to have taken precedence over clarity.

11. Consideration of Consistency, Responsibility, and Normative Coherence
Official statements issued by major states may have a substantial influence on the practical direction of the application of international law and on the manner in which such application is perceived by the international community. Accordingly, it is desirable, in principle, that such statements reflect the overall structure of the conflict, apply legal standards consistently to all relevant actors, and provide for comprehensive consideration of responsibility structures.
Where only certain acts are emphasized, while the same legal standards are not clearly applied to other significant related acts, questions may arise regarding the consistency of the application of international law, the perception of selective application, and the maintenance of neutrality and normative credibility.
International peace is strengthened not through the selective evaluation of particular actors, but through an approach that applies the same standards to all relevant conduct and seeks to record responsibility wherever it exists. In that sense, such statements may themselves constitute important records in the future review of responsibility, judgment, and institutional seriousness.

13. Consideration of Responsibility and Implications
The following issues may arise for future consideration.
① Possible Responsibility in Relation to Escalation
Under Article 33 of the United Nations Charter, parties to a dispute are under an obligation to seek peaceful settlement. Where diplomatic balance is not sufficiently maintained in an unstable situation, such an approach may become subject to future review in connection with increased tensions or the risk of escalation.

② Consistency in the Application of International Humanitarian Law
The protection of civilians and humanitarian principles must be applied consistently in all conflict situations. Where there is a marked disparity between the application of such principles in one situation and their application in another comparable situation, further review may be warranted with respect to the universality and consistency of the application of international law.

③ Consideration of State Responsibility and Economic Impact
Under international law, where the conduct of a state, or coordinated conduct among states, has a substantial economic impact on third states, such consequences may be reviewed within the framework of state responsibility. In particular, where international policy positions or joint official acts have materially affected market and economic structures, such effects may become subject to future review concerning responsibility structures and impact implications.
This joint statement is not merely a diplomatic expression. It constitutes an official policy act formed through the approval and judgment of each participating decision-maker. Accordingly, the international effects arising from it cannot be regarded as entirely separable from the judgment of those decision-makers who endorsed it. Responsibility may therefore be considered not only at the institutional level but also at the level of the exercise of individual decision-making authority.
Moreover, where conduct that may constitute an internationally wrongful act is recognized, yet meaningful efforts to prevent, balance, or mediate such conduct are lacking, such omission may, in certain circumstances, be reviewed not as neutrality but as a form of passive responsibility carrying legal significance. In particular, where silence, asymmetric responses, or selective responses have the practical effect of creating an environment more conducive to escalation, such matters may become subject to deeper review.

14. Domestic Constitutional Foundations and Democratic Legitimacy
The constitutional orders of the participating states are generally founded upon the principle that public authority derives from the people. By way of example, the United Kingdom relies on constitutional convention and the principle of parliamentary sovereignty; France is founded on the constitutional principle that national sovereignty belongs to the people; Germany on the Basic Law principle that all state authority emanates from the people; Italy on the constitutional principle that sovereignty belongs to the people; Japan on the constitutional principle of popular sovereignty; Canada on constitutional democracy and parliamentary legitimacy; and the Netherlands on democratic constitutional order and the rule of law. The constitutional provisions of France, Germany, and Italy each expressly set forth the democratic foundations of popular sovereignty and state authority.
This means that the exercise of authority by state leaders is not merely a matter of power, but of fiduciary responsibility delegated by the people. Such trust is most faithfully discharged where legal standards are applied with greater clarity, consistency, and principled commitment, even in situations that may be diplomatically uncomfortable.

15. Integrated Principle of Authority and Responsibility
The authority exercised by those representing a state is, domestically, a fiduciary authority delegated by the people and, internationally, a responsibility-bearing authority that must be exercised within a legal order directed toward peace, stability, and accountability.
Accordingly, where state leaders participate in a joint statement capable of affecting international security, markets, and the welfare of third states, such participation may be reviewed under two corresponding standards: domestic constitutional legitimacy and international responsibility.
In particular, high public office should be understood not merely as a position from which one may speak, but as a position in which one is expected to bear responsibility for the foreseeable legal and material consequences of one’s choices.

16. Consideration of the Omission of an Assessment of Aggression
The joint statement under review refers to various principles of international law, yet it does not expressly address one of the most fundamental issues within the international legal order: the assessment of aggression.
Article 2(4) of the United Nations Charter establishes the prohibition of the use of force as a foundational norm of the international legal order. Within that structure, the question of aggression often constitutes the key point of departure for determining the starting point of an armed conflict, the foundational basis for the allocation of responsibility among actors, and the completeness and consistency of the application of international law.

Accordingly, where an official statement concerning an ongoing conflict does not expressly address whether aggression has occurred, additional review may be warranted as to whether the initial cause of the armed conflict has been sufficiently considered, whether the responsibility structure has been established upon an appropriate point of departure, and whether international law has been applied in a structurally complete and balanced manner.
Where the review of aggression is absent, while other conduct is selectively emphasized, this may give rise to legitimate discussion concerning the structural completeness, neutrality, and normative courage of the statement. For that reason, the absence of a review of aggression should be preserved not merely as a drafting omission, but as an element possessing independent significance for future international review.
This document objectively records such omission and preserves it for reference in future legal and institutional review.

18. Consideration of War-Linked Gain Structures and the Possibility of Risk Conversion
In relation to the present matter, policy-oriented approaches have already been proposed that seek to identify so-called “war-linked gain structures,” including economic gains, increases in maritime logistics costs, rising insurance premiums, and fluctuations in energy prices that may arise during armed conflict or periods of escalating tension, and to convert such structures into risk-based frameworks.
Such approaches provide a framework for understanding economic flows linked to war or escalation not merely as market phenomena, but as public risk structures that may be tracked, classified, and, under certain conditions, examined for possible restriction or recovery. In particular, such structures may include elements such as review of the formation of war-related premiums, identification of asset flows and network-based risk signals, restriction of liquidity movements and classification of risk assets, and the possibility of victim-centered recovery and allocation.
These approaches do not directly create new legal obligations; rather, they provide a policy and technical basis for identifying and responding to risk at an early stage within the framework of existing international law and the authority of states. However, it is not clearly established within the scope of this document how, if at all, such consideration of war-linked gain structures and approaches to risk conversion were taken into account in actual policy decision-making or in the formation of the Joint Statement.
In the future, the following may become subjects of review: the existence of economic gain structures arising in the course of war or escalating tension; the possibility of prior identification of and response to such structures; and the extent to which such structures may have influenced the continuation or escalation of the conflict.
In particular, where economic gains linked to war are formed and maintained through a certain structure, whether and how such structures were addressed may become an important factor in the review of the development of the conflict and its consequences.

In this connection, the author of this document formally submitted to the United Nations the War-Linked Asset Management & Maritime Logistics Stabilization Package in High-Risk Geopolitical Instability (WARM-P) on 3 March 2026 (KST), and the relevant materials are preserved in a separate attachment folder.

This structure includes a policy and operational framework capable of restraining escalation while preserving international economic and humanitarian stability through the protection of civilian survival logistics, the guarantee of neutral transit, and an operational system separated from military involvement. In particular, this approach provides a pathway through which states may fulfill a certain level of international responsibility without military participation, while also making it possible to pursue both de-escalation between the parties to the conflict and the protection of supply chains.
