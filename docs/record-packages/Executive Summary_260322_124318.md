Executive Summary

Submitted by: Jungyun Kwon, Citizen of the Republic of Korea and Global Citizen
Date: March 22, 2026 (KST)

This memorandum provides a neutral record based on international principles, focusing on consistency and responsibility regarding the Joint Statement issued on 19 March 2026 concerning the Strait of Hormuz. It has been prepared from the perspective of a global citizen and is intended to serve as an objective record for potential institutional review, policy evaluation, or legal consideration.

The Joint Statement reflects concern regarding maritime security, freedom of navigation, and risks to energy supply chains. However, this record identifies that its treatment of the broader conflict structure appears limited. In particular, the assessment of prior uses of force, the interaction of multiple actors, and the allocation of responsibility across those actors is not comprehensively reflected within the statement.

The document places emphasis on the consequences of maritime disruption, including risks to shipping and global energy flows. At the same time, the underlying causes of these developments, the processes through which escalation occurred, and the extent to which diplomatic, mediatory, or fact-finding efforts were undertaken to address these causes are not clearly presented in the text. This results in a structure where outcomes are highlighted, while the causal chain and mitigation efforts remain less visible.

In addition, the absence of an explicit assessment of aggression, together with the limited consideration of external harm to third states, suggests that the application of international legal principles may not be fully comprehensive. These elements may be relevant in evaluating the consistency and completeness of the legal framework reflected in the statement.

It is also noted that, prior to the issuance of the Joint Statement, several non-military and structurally viable policy frameworks had already been proposed. These include approaches addressing war-linked economic incentives, the quantification and allocation of external harm, the protection of civilian survival logistics through neutral transit mechanisms, and integrated systems for conflict containment and accountability. The existence of such alternatives may be relevant in assessing whether a broader range of policy options was available at the time of decision-making.

From a consistency and responsibility perspective, the presence of feasible alternatives, combined with a focus on specific outcomes rather than the full causal and responsibility structure, may give rise to questions regarding the completeness and uniformity of international application. At the same time, this memorandum does not advance a political position, but rather preserves elements that may be relevant for future examination.

This document is based exclusively on publicly available information and applicable international principles. It is intended as a record at a specific point in time and may be updated should additional factual information or relevant materials become available.
