Annex IV.
Damage Assessment Standard
for External Harm in International Conflicts

Submitted by: Jungyun Kwon, Citizen of the Republic of Korea and Global Citizen
Date: March 6, 2026 (KST)

Article 1
Purpose
The purpose of this Standard is to establish methodologies for the objective assessment of economic, environmental, supply-chain and social harm affecting third States arising from international conflicts.

This Standard provides a common framework for the quantitative evaluation of external harm within an international cooperative system.

Article 2
Guiding Principles
Damage assessment shall be conducted in accordance with the following principles:
1. Objective data-based assessment
2. International comparability
3. Transparent calculation methods
4. Protection of third States affected by conflict

Article 3
Categories of Harm
External harm shall be classified into the following categories:
1. Economic harm
2. Supply-chain harm
3. Environmental harm
4. Financial system harm
5. Food and energy harm
6. Humanitarian harm

Article 3-2
Harm Caused by Proxy Forces
Where conflict-related harm is caused by non-state actors or proxy forces, such harm shall also be included in the external harm assessment.

The following elements may be recorded during the assessment process:
1. Direct actor
2. Existence of a supporting State
3. Type of support
4. Pathway through which the harm occurred
Such information may be used as reference material in responsibility scoring and liability allocation processes.

Article 4
Economic Harm Assessment
Economic harm shall be assessed on the basis of the following factors:
1. Reduction in gross domestic product
2. Reduction in trade
3. Decline in industrial production
4. Decline in investment
The basic formula may be expressed as follows:
Economic Damage = (Baseline Economic Indicator − Observed Indicator) × Impact Duration

Exchange Rate and Inflation Adjustment
In order to reflect the effects of exchange rate fluctuations and inflation in protracted conflicts, the following factors may be taken into consideration:
• Conversion into an international reference currency
• Consumer Price Index (CPI) or equivalent inflation indicators
• Purchasing Power Parity (PPP) adjustments
Where appropriate, assessed damages may be adjusted in real terms.

Article 5
Supply-Chain Harm Assessment
Supply-chain harm may include the following factors:
1. Logistics delays
2. Increased transportation costs
3. Disruptions in critical component supply
4. Production interruptions

Reference Data
• Global maritime freight indices
• International logistics indicators

Article 6
Financial System Harm Assessment
Financial system harm shall be assessed on the basis of the following factors:
1. Increased financial market volatility
2. Capital outflows
3. Currency depreciation
4. Financial system instability

Reference Data
• Global financial indices
• Foreign exchange market data

Article 7
Food and Energy Harm Assessment
Food and energy harm may include the following factors:
1. Increase in energy prices
2. Increase in food prices
3. Disruption of energy supply
4. Reduction in food supply

Reference Data
• International crude oil prices
• Global grain price indices

Article 8
Environmental Harm Assessment
Environmental harm may include the following factors:
1. Ecosystem damage
2. Spread of pollution
3. Loss of natural resources
4. Environmental restoration costs
5. Greenhouse gas emissions arising from military activities
Environmental costs associated with greenhouse gas emissions may be quantitatively assessed using international carbon price indicators or comparable environmental valuation benchmarks.

Article 9
Humanitarian Harm
Humanitarian harm may include:
1. Civilian casualties
2. Collapse or overload of medical systems
3. Population displacement and refugee movements
4. Increased humanitarian assistance costs

Article 10
Data Sources
The assessment of harm may draw upon the following data sources:
1. International market data
2. Economic statistics
3. Supply-chain indicators
4. Environmental data
5. Satellite imagery
6. Maritime logistics data
7. Air transport data
8. Humanitarian harm and displacement data
9. Medical and public health impact data

Article 11
Baseline Setting
For purposes of harm assessment, a pre-conflict baseline shall be established.
The baseline may be determined with reference to:
1. Average economic indicators prior to the conflict
2. Long-term market averages
3. Regional comparative data

Article 12
Damage Period
Damage assessment shall be conducted with reference to the following periods:
1. The period during which the conflict occurs
2. The period during which the conflict’s effects persist
3. The recovery period

Article 13
Elimination of Double Counting
Adjustment procedures shall be applied to prevent the same harm from being counted multiple times across different categories.

Article 14
Aggregation of Harm
1.  Harm in each category shall be aggregated through an integrated harm assessment model.

The total amount of external harm may be expressed as follows:
Total Harm = Economic Harm + Supply-Chain Harm + Financial System Harm + Environmental Harm + Food and Energy Harm + Humanitarian Harm

2. Where harm occurs in the course of self-defense or responsive actions, such harm may be assessed in light of the following criteria:
• Whether the action constituted a legitimate response to an attack
• Whether the principle of proportionality was observed
• Whether harm to third States occurred

Where an action is determined to constitute a legitimate exercise of self-defense, the action itself shall not be treated as a basis for responsibility attribution.
However, harm inflicted upon third States as a result of such action may still be included in the external harm assessment.

Article 15
Application of Responsibility Shares
The total external harm assessed pursuant to this Standard may be allocated among relevant actors according to responsibility shares calculated under the Responsibility Scoring Framework.

This process shall be carried out in accordance with the Responsibility Scoring Framework.

Where any of the following circumstances are identified during the calculation of responsibility shares, additional weighting factors may be considered:
1. Conflict decisions taken for personal political gain
2. Conflict initiation motivated by economic or financial private interests
3. Approval of attacks despite awareness of possible violations of international law
4. Approval of operations despite disregard for known risks to civilians
Such circumstances may be evaluated in conjunction with Article 16 of the Responsibility Scoring Framework.

Article 16
Data Verification
Data used for the assessment of harm shall be subject to verification through the following procedures:
1. Cross-data analysis
2. Independent expert review
3. Comparison with international datasets

Article 17
Long-Term Harm Assessment
Where the following long-term effects are identified in the process of external harm assessment, such effects may be recorded as long-term impacts:
1. Environmental and ecological damage
2. Long-term economic decline
3. Population displacement and refugee-related impacts
4. Structural changes in supply chains
Such impacts may be assessed further in annual reports or in dedicated long-term analytical reports.

Article 18
Reporting
The results of harm assessment shall be included in the Annual Global External Harm Report.

Article 19
Methodological Development
The harm assessment methodology may be continuously improved through:
1. Use of new data sources
2. International cooperative research
3. Technological advancement
