Annex III.
Responsibility Scoring Framework
for External Harm in International Conflicts

Submitted by: Jungyun Kwon, Citizen of the Republic of Korea and Global Citizen
Date: March 6, 2026 (KST)

Article 1
Purpose
The purpose of this Regulation is to establish a scoring framework for assessing the relative contribution to external harm affecting third States arising from international conflicts.

This framework does not aim to determine political responsibility but rather to provide an analytical model for evaluating the relative contribution of conflict-related actions to external harm.

Article 2
Guiding Principles
Responsibility scoring shall be conducted in accordance with the following principles:
1. Objective data-based assessment
2. Maintenance of political neutrality
3. Respect for principles of international law
4. Protection of third States affected by conflict

Article 3
Actions Subject to Assessment
Actions subject to responsibility assessment may include conflict-related activities such as:
1. Armed conflict
2. Actions causing civilian harm or damage to civilian infrastructure
3. Cyber operations
4. Economic blockades or coercive economic restrictions
5. Interference with financial systems
6. Restrictions affecting energy supply
7. Disruption of supply chains

Article 4
Structure of the Responsibility Score
Responsibility scores shall be composed of the following components:
1. Initial Escalation Score (IES)
2. Operational Impact Score (OIS)
3. Conflict Expansion Score (CES)
4. Civilian Harm Impact Score (CHIS)
5. External Harm Impact Score (EHIS)

The final responsibility score for each actor shall be calculated as follows:
RSₐ = IESₐ + OISₐ + CESₐ + CHISₐ + EHISₐ − DADJₐ
Where:
• RSₐ = final responsibility score of actor a
• IESₐ = initial escalation score
• OISₐ = operational impact score
• CESₐ = conflict expansion score
• CHISₐ = civilian harm impact score
• EHISₐ = external harm impact score
• DADJₐ = defensive action adjustment

The total responsibility score of all participating actors shall be calculated as:
• TRS = Σ RSₐ

Article 4-2
Proxy Actions and Support Responsibility
Where conflict-related actions are conducted by non-state actors or proxy forces, and such actions are assessed to have been enabled through substantial support by a State, a proportion of responsibility may be attributed to the supporting State.

Support responsibility may take into consideration the following factors:
1. Provision of military equipment or weapons
2. Financial support
3. Military training or operational assistance
4. Intelligence sharing or command coordination
5. Logistical or strategic support

In such cases, the responsibility score of the supporting State may be calculated as:
RS_support = RS_proxy × SF

Where:
• RS_support = responsibility score of supporting State
• RS_proxy = responsibility score of proxy actor
• SF = Support Factor
• Support Factor range: 0.05 – 0.40

Article 4-3
Structure of Proxy Responsibility
Where external harm results from actions conducted by proxy forces or non-state actors, the following responsibility structure may apply:
1. Primary responsibility for external harm compensation shall be attributed to the State where substantial support is identified.
2. Responsibility may be recognized where the State has provided:
• Military support
• Financial support
• Operational assistance
• Territorial access
• Tolerance or protection
3.   Proxy actors or related individuals may be subject to     
      secondary accountability measures including:
• Criminal responsibility
• Asset freezing
• Asset recovery
• Sanctions
4.    Where a State has fulfilled compensation obligations, it 
        may exercise recourse rights against proxy actors or 
        related individuals.

Article 4-4
Multiple Support Structures
Where proxy forces receive support from multiple States, the contribution of each supporting State may be evaluated based on:
1. Scale of support
2. Duration of support
3. Operational influence
4. Degree of strategic control

The responsibility score of each supporting State may be calculated as:
• RS_supportᵢ = RS_proxy × SFᵢ
Where SFᵢ represents the support contribution factor of State i.

Article 5
Initial Escalation Score
The Initial Escalation Score may consider factors such as:
1. Initiation of direct military attacks
2. Military or non-military actions likely to induce escalation
3. Existence of prior warning or de-escalation measures
4. Reasonable attempts at diplomatic resolution

Escalation-inducing actions prior to the outbreak of conflict may be incorporated into the score.

The Initial Escalation Score may be structured as:
• IES = DI + EI − DE
Where:
• DI = Direct Initiation
• EI = Escalation Inducement
• DE = De-escalation Efforts
• Score range: 0 – 40

Article 5-2
Self-Defense and Responsive Actions
States retain the right to exercise self-defense under international law.

Defensive actions meeting the following criteria shall not be treated as adverse factors in responsibility scoring:
1. Response to an actual attack
2. Compliance with the principle of proportionality
3. Actions directed against the attacking party

However, responsibility scoring may consider such actions where:
1. Defensive actions cause significant harm to third States
2. Civilian protection principles are clearly violated
3. Proportionality is clearly exceeded

In such cases, impacts may be reflected in:
• External Harm Impact Score
• Civilian Harm Impact Score

Article 6
Operational Impact Score
Operational Impact Score evaluates the effects of conflict actions on global systems.

Evaluation factors may include:
1. Energy market impact
2. Financial market impact
3. Supply chain disruption
4. Food system impact
5. Critical civilian infrastructure impact
Score range: 0 – 30

Article 7
Conflict Expansion Score
Conflict expansion may increase the scale of external harm.

Evaluation factors include:
1. Expansion of military operations
2. Participation of additional States
3. Expansion of economic or financial measures
Score range: 0 – 20

Article 8
External Harm Impact Score
This score reflects actual damage experienced by third States.

Evaluation factors include:
1. Economic damage
2. Supply chain disruption
3. Environmental damage
4. Food and energy impacts
Score range: 0 – 30

Article 9
Civilian Harm Impact Score
Civilian harm impact shall be evaluated based on:
1. Scale of civilian casualties
2. Damage to civilian infrastructure
3. Impact on medical and humanitarian systems
4. Population displacement or refugee flows
Score range: 0 – 30

Article 10
Defensive Action Adjustment
Defensive actions may be considered as adjustment factors where the following conditions are satisfied:
1. Compliance with international humanitarian law
2. Protection of civilians
3. Observance of proportionality
4. Evidence of efforts to minimize civilian harm
Adjustment range: maximum reduction of 15 points

Article 11
Allocation of Responsibility in Multi-Actor Conflicts
Where multiple actors participate in a conflict, responsibility scores shall be allocated based on:
1. Level of military participation
2. Participation in economic or coercive measures
3. Operational contribution

Article 12
Responsibility Share
Responsibility shares shall be calculated based on the total responsibility score of all participating actors.

Actors whose responsibility share falls below 5% may be classified as de minimis contributors.

Such actors may be excluded from detailed allocation calculations but may remain referenced in analytical reports.

Example:
A: 60 points
B: 30 points
C: 10 points
Total: 100

Responsibility share:
A – 60%
B – 30%
C – 10%

Article 13
Harm Allocation
Responsibility shares shall be applied proportionally to the total external harm arising from a conflict.
Affected States shall submit the total scale of external harm without the requirement to identify specific responsible States.

Article 14
Data Sources
Responsibility scoring may rely on various objective datasets including:
1. International market data
2. Supply chain indicators
3. Energy and food price indices
4. Environmental damage data
5. Satellite imagery
6. Real-time maritime shipping data (AIS)
7. Global logistics and aviation transport data
8. Civilian harm and humanitarian impact datasets

Article 15
Verification
Responsibility scoring shall be reviewed by an Independent Verification Panel.
Verification procedures may include:
1. Cross-validation of data
2. Model verification
3. Expert assessment

Article 16
Principle of Decision-Maker Responsibility
Compensation for external harm shall in principle be implemented by the State associated with the relevant conflict actions.

Where the following conditions are identified, additional responsibility review may be conducted regarding decision-makers or relevant individuals:
1. Pursuit of personal political gain in conflict decisions
2. Initiation of conflict for economic benefit
3. Decisions involving attacks prohibited under international law
4. Authorization of attacks despite known risks to civilians

Article 17
Influence of Economic Actors
Where the activities of private corporations or economic actors contribute to external harm during a conflict, such activities may be documented, including:
1. Provision of military equipment or technology
2. Supply of strategic resources
3. Logistical support
4. Financial assistance
Such information may be considered in analytical assessments of responsibility.

Article 18
Cost Recovery from Responsible Individuals
Following external harm compensation, Member States may consider cost recovery from responsible individuals through:
1. Domestic judicial proceedings
2. International legal procedures
3. Asset freezing or recovery mechanisms

Article 19
Reporting
Results of responsibility scoring analyses shall be included in the Annual Global External Harm Report.

Article 20
Model Development
The assessment model may be continuously refined through:
1. New data sources
2. International collaborative research
3. Expert review processes
